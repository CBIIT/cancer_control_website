Welcome to Informatics for Consumer Health

Posted on 04-28-2010

On behalf of the Informatics for Consumer Health partnering organizations, welcome to InformaticsforConsumerHealth.org! This online resource aims to assist in the exchange of ideas to bridge the gap between health care and information technology.

InformaticsforConsumerHealth.org hopes to serve as a platform for communication and collaboration among various stakeholders. Improvements in health care must be built upon a foundation of enhanced communication and collaboration. Health by its very nature is a collaborative enterprise. It can be influenced by the ways in which consumers/patients and physicians communicate with each other; by the ability or inability of physicians to share vital medical information across offices in timely and reliable ways; and by the ability of consumers to gain mastery over their own health through the support of employers, public service organizations, government, and private industry. Health information technology, or health informatics, will serve as the foundation for connecting and enabling this collaborative community. But unless thought is given to the ways in which stakeholders coordinate their efforts, consumers will be lost to a cacophony of self-interest, indecipherable data, and conflicting messages.

For these reasons InformaticsForConsumerHealth.org was developed to bring various stakeholders across sectors together and set a path for coordination, quality, and future opportunity. Provided are the most recent news and research to keep the community updated on evidence-based innovations and products in-development, as well as funding and collaborative opportunities available from a variety of sources. And most importantly, invested communities, will be able to exchange ideas and access information that will help empower providers and consumers to manage health care.

Show Me the Data: Visualizing and Applying What We Know About Our Health

Posted on 10-04-2010
By: Lila J. Rutten, Ph.D., M.P.H.
Behavioral Scientist, Clinical Monitoring Research Program
SAIC-Frederick, Inc.
National Cancer Institute at Frederick
Abdul R. Shaikh, Ph.D., MHSc
Program Director, Health Communication & Informatics Research Branch
National Cancer Institute

The National Cancer Institute’s (NCI) Division of Cancer Control and Population Sciences (DCCPS) is pleased to share our recently issued challenge, Enabling Community Use of Data for Cancer Prevention and Control. Associated with the Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) Community Health Data Initiative (CHDI), the challenge encourages interdisciplinary teams of entrepreneurs, developers, and health scientists to develop innovative applications that use publically available data, and build upon the behavioral and communication science evidence-base, to deliver population health data to communities to inform cancer prevention and control.

CHDI comes out of the 2009 Open Government Directive, which promotes the Administration's emphasis on transparency, participation and collaboration in government, in sync with the Web 2.0 zeitgeist of online information sharing and collaboration. The ascendance of online platforms for communication and collaboration underscores the need for HHS to better understand the online flow of information for health communication. Health 2.0 can be described as the translation/application of Web 2.0 to medicine and public health, spawning discussion of concepts such as "apomediaries" whereby traditional gatekeepers of information such as health professionals are replaced by individuals in online networks who strive to provide the wisdom of crowds (be it right or wrong). NCI is focused on staying abreast of such trends in health informatics with conceptual frameworks such as the PopSciGrid and the Grid Enabled Measures Portal that embrace the data-sharing and collaborative potential of the Internet for cancer treatment, prevention and control.

We were particularly excited to see HHS launch the CHDI earlier this year because of its emphasis on catalyzing community health "data suppliers" and "data appliers" to find innovative ways to use health-related data for community impact. In a similar vein our Division supports a diverse portfolio of research on the science of communicating health-related data. For instance, the NCI Centers of Excellence in Cancer Communication Research (CECCR) reflect the Institute's recognition that effective communication can help narrow the gap between bench-to-trench research discoveries and application for cancer prevention and control. As the health 2.0 landscape continues to evolve, we're excited not only to contribute to an initiative advancing evidence-based research but use it to fuel innovation.
We are enthusiastic about the challenge and we are hopeful that innovative entrepreneurs and health scientists will submit creative applications for harmonizing and visualizing health-related data to enable and incentivize communities to utilize cancer data. We look forward to sharing the winning applications at the Hawaii International Conference on System Sciences workshop: Designing Community Health Systems: Opportunities and Challenges in Communicating Data.

Thanks,
Lila & Abdul
You can find more information about the challenge at Health 2.0 and Challenge.gov!

By: Audie Atienza, Ph.D.

Like Wow!!

High-level panel sessions with VERY distinguished speakers will delve into the topics of Cross-Sectoral Perspectives, Future Visions, Comparative Effectiveness Research, Lessons Learned Across the Globe, CEO/Executive Perspectives, Privacy/Security, and Regulatory/Policy Issues. We anticipate 2,000 participants at the 2010 Summit from many different sectors of the mHealth ecosystem. Exploring the state of the science in mHealth, interacting with cool mobile tools, and engaging in unprecedented network opportunities across different sectors… you do not want to miss the 2010 mHealth Summit!

Addressing Health Literacy in the Digital Age

Posted on 01-03-2011
By: Jessica Mark, M.P.H., CPH, Health Communication and ehealth Service Fellow

Linda Harris, Ph.D., Lead, Health Communication and ehealth Team
Office of Disease Prevention and Health Promotion
U.S. Department of Health and Human Services
Nearly half of all American adults—90 million people—have difficulty understanding and acting upon basic health information needed to make appropriate health decisions.1

Low health literacy has been linked to inappropriate use of preventive services and screenings, higher rates of hospitalization, and poorer reported health status.2 As public and private institutions are increasingly using the Internet and other technologies to streamline the delivery of health information and services, another layer of complexity is added to the skills required to effectively manage one’s health. The combination of low health literacy and low digital literacy together may be a barrier to equitable health outcomes on the population level. The U.S. Department of Health and Human Services’ Office of Disease Prevention and Health Promotion (ODPHP) is involved in several initiatives to provide leadership and guidance in improving both health literacy and digital literacy across the nation.

As more and more health information and services move online, health literacy in a digital world requires consumers to not only find, evaluate, understand and use health information; but also to understand and navigate the technology being used. Improving health literacy is about much more than just simplifying words. However, the broad-based application of evidence-based best practices in user-centered design, Web design principles and clear writing together provide a necessary foundation for progress. There must be a widespread movement to provide health content, tools and resources online and in emerging digital technologies that are more accessible to low health and digital literacy populations.

Several recent national policies and initiatives have brought attention to health and digital literacy, and catalyzed the public health and consumer informatics communities in addressing these issues. These initiatives include the recently enacted health care reform legislation (Affordable Care Act); the National Action Plan to Improve Health Literacy; and the recent formation of a federal Interagency Digital Literacy Working Group.

The Affordable Care Act (ACA) is at the forefront of the nation’s domestic policy agenda. The Institute of Medicine Roundtable on Health Literacy recently convened a Health Literacy and Health Care Reform Workshop to discuss health literacy provisions in the ACA. The ACA includes a number of windows of opportunity for addressing health and digital literacy, including references to the provision of quality patient information in both print and electronic media, as well as improved nutrition labeling and design and presentation of prescription label information.3 With regard to consumer health informatics, the ACA also frequently prescribes the use of technology for engaging the public in health-improving interactions.

The National Action Plan to Improve Health Literacy identifies overarching goals and high priority strategies for creating a health literate society. The vision to achieving this plan is dependent on seven goals, including developing and disseminating health and safety information that is accurate, accessible, and actionable. Implicit in this goal is the need to make information accessible—regardless of internet experience, comfort with technology and online search and navigation skills.

Finally, the Interagency Digital Literacy Working Group is committed to spurring the advancement of digital literacy across all age groups and stages of life. The Working Group was established by The Subcommittee on Broadband of the National Science and Technology Council’s Committee on Technology. The Working Group is planning the launch of an online resource to provide an archive of resources related to improving digital literacy. This repository will include a coherent collection of tightly related and relevant resources on digital literacy across government agencies. The group is working to ensure that individual digital literacy education is complemented by organizational and institutional shifts toward Web design and content that better meets the needs of consumers.
One way we aim to improve health and digital literacy via quality health information and design is to provide best practice guidance to other intermediary organizations with Health Literacy Online: A guide to writing and designing easy-to-use health Website s. Health Literacy Online is a research-based how-to guide for creating health Website s and Web content for the millions of Americans with limited literacy skills and limited experience using the Web. The strategies in this guide complement accepted principles of good Web design and thus have the potential to improve the online experience for all users, regardless of literacy skills. The guide offers an overview of how to deliver online health information that is actionable and engaging, create a health Website that's easy to use, and evaluate and improve a health Web site with user-centered design.

The Health Literacy Online guide was developed through the course of the redesign of healthfinder.gov and its Quick Guide to Healthy Living, a resource for actionable prevention and wellness information and tools. 15 studies over a period of 4 years informed the development of the Quick Guide and the redesign of healthfinder.gov overall. More than 750 people aged 18 to 84 participated in these studies, and special care was taken to recruit and test adults with limited literacy skills. The Quick Guide and healthfinder.gov serve as a living example of the very principles outlined in the Health Literacy Online guide. Now, healthfinder.gov is featured as the go-to prevention resource on healthcare.gov, continuing the tradition of reaching those with low health literacy and limited Web skills.
The guidelines espoused by Health Literacy Online and embodied in the Quick Guide to Healthy Living are now also being used as part of the criteria for judging the myHealthyPeople Application Developer Challenge. The challenge, conducted in collaboration with Health 2.0, will award prizes to successful mobile and/or web applications that make it easy for stakeholders of Healthy People to keep up with the initiative and combine related data in meaningful ways—within a user-centered interface design.

We hope to bridge the worlds of health literacy and digital literacy, and to integrate these goals into the healthcare reform movement. We hope you will join us in creating a more accessible digital environment that consumers of all health and digital literacy levels can enjoy and use to support better health decisions, and in empowering citizens to take control of their health—through healthcare reform and beyond.

For more information, please visit:

  • Health Literacy Online: A guide to writing and designing easy-to-use health Website s
  • myHealthyPeople Challenge

Health Literacy Research that Contributes to Effective Consumer Health Informatics

Posted on 02-02-2011
Cynthia Baur, Ph.D.
Senior Advisor, Health Literacy
Office of the Associate Director for Communication
Centers for Disease Control and Prevention

Improving health literacy is a national public health imperative. The January 3, 2011, blog posting on health literacy in the digital age summarizes several initiatives and events that have propelled health literacy improvement onto the national stage. The National Action Plan to Improve Health Literacy is one of those initiatives and includes seven goals and multiple strategies, many relevant to the consumer health informatics research agenda. In particular, one goal includes strategies on making health information and recommendations clear and actionable, and another goal addresses the need for more research and evaluation, including ehealth applications, to improve health literacy.

Health literacy research is supported in large part by the program announcement from the National Institutes for Health (NIH), Understanding and Promoting Health Literacy. The Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality (AHRQ) and the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) also participate in the program announcement. In addition to supporting research, AHRQ created Accessible Health Information Technology (IT) for Populations with Limited Literacy: A Guide for Developers and Purchasers of Health IT. (PDF) The combination of the Action Plan, research projects, and the developers' and purchasers' guide creates an opportunity for consumer health informatics researchers and developers to join with their health literacy colleagues to create truly accessible digital applications for consumers.

NIH-supported health literacy research projects that directly involve informatics include

  • computer-based education and self-management for chronic diseases, e.g., An RCT to Reduce Cardiopulmonary Re-Hospitalizations or Health Literacy, Technology Acceptance, On-line Self Care: Understanding Teens
  • Internet access and information-seeking skills, e.g., Click to Connect: Improving Health Literacy through Internet Literacy
  • virtual experiences of probabilities, e.g., Communicating Probabilities through Interactive Computer Graphics
  • effective speech techniques for multimedia, e.g., Evaluation of Clear Speech to Enhance Multimedia eHealth
  • personally controlled health records, e.g., Health Literacy and Information Management in ADHD: Designing an Optimal Record
  • patient education in electronic health records, e.g., Promoting Health Literacy for Newly Prescribed via the EMR
  • computerized tool to generate "plain language," e.g., Improving Health Outcomes through Computer Generation of Reader-Appropriate Texts.

In each of these examples, investigators are focused on populations with limited health literacy and one or more health challenges. Technology is used as an intervention or part of a communication strategy, such as spoken plain language or interactive graphics, to improve understanding. The findings from these studies are useful not only for other health literacy projects but also for informatics researchers and developers who want to design applications for specific populations with limited health literacy skills or address limited health literacy when they design and test applications for mass markets.

Other non-technology health literacy studies have insights for informatics researchers and developers. Several NIH funded studies focus on special populations, such as teens or older adults, and their health literacy skills. Understanding the differences in the two groups' motivations to seek health information as well as their interest in different presentation formats is valuable information for application development. Several other studies examine how health literacy skills are measured. The findings from these studies can help with specific data on health literacy skills than the national data in the National Assessment of Adult Literacy (NAAL) and provide questionnaires and other research instruments for informatics studies.

In addition to the Health Literacy Online guide mentioned in the January 3 blog, AHRQ's Developers' and Purchasers' Guide is a succinct summary of key design principles that consider health literacy skills and type of technology. This guidance can help researchers and developers systematically assess their attention to health literacy issues. AHRQ also has an evidence report on health IT use by the elderly, chronically ill and underserved populations that summarizes the functions with the most positive effects. CDC has plain language guidance that researchers and developers can use to create text and graphics that are clear and accessible, and NIH has plain language tips and other clear communication information.

The Institute of Medicine report on health literacy explicitly made the point that designers of health information and services must change their practices and use health literacy principles to create more accessible products. Technology can be a great asset to improved accessibility, comprehension, and health management skills. Digital health information and services can provide freedom and control when consumers and patients can use technology to manage their health when, where and how they want. If digital information and services are too jargon-filled or complex, however, technology becomes one more barrier for consumers and patients to overcome. Consumer health informatics experts can apply the insights from health literacy research and guidance to make sure that digital health achieves its promise and avoids frustrating consumers or worse, making them indifferent to e-health's benefits.

Informatics for Consumer Health: A Core Function for the National Library of Medicine

Posted on 02-02-2011
Milton Corn, M.D.
Deputy Director for Research and Education
Acting Director, Office of Health Information Programs and Development
National Library of Medicine
National Institutes of Health

The National Library of Medicine (NLM) is the world's largest biomedical library, and the central focus of its mission is information: its collection, storage, accessibility, and maximally efficient utilization. The Library has been a pioneer in recognizing the value of information technology, and in the adoption and development of informatics. NLM provides essential information and tools in support of research by biologists and clinical researchers, but considers of equal importance the needs of health care providers, and consumers, the twin targets of the Informatics for Consumer Health initiative. A significant fraction of NLM resources are dedicated to consumer informatics, which is an important component of NLM's online resources, its intramural research, and its grant and contract funding opportunities for the extramural community.

Consumer Information is provided directly to millions by NLM through MedlinePlus. Developed as the National Institutes of Health's consumer-friendly website for patients and their families and friends, it also has become a popular stop for clinicians seeking basic information or a refresher about a broad variety of topics relevant to health and disease. Conversely, PubMed, another NLM database comprising more than 20 million citations for biomedical literature and intended primarily for professionals, is open to the public and receives much use from those consumers who are undaunted by the jargon inevitable in professional writing.
Intramural Research at NLM also is centered on informatics and currently includes such consumer informatics projects as decision support for clinicians, and personal health records for consumers.

Extramural Funding from the Library's grant and contract programs has long been an important source of support for projects relevant to consumer informatics. Past programs include telemedicine, awards to provide internet access to the underserved, and the development of Web resources for consumers on such topics as mental health, drug information, multiple sclerosis, and care of the elderly among others.

Current grant programs continue to have biomedical informatics as a central element, and can be utilized by those whose interest is consumer health informatics.

NLM offers a broad spectrum of grant opportunities including traditional NIH research awards for investigator-originated ideas or for announced themes of interest to NIH such as literacy or behavior, resource awards for online information sources, training in informatics, conference awards, and support for small businesses.

Research Support is available as R01 awards. Suitable research topics among others are:

  • Visualization and presentation approaches for information: e.g., to enhance decisions, learning or understanding, including intelligent agents, customizable interfaces
  • Analysis and evaluation of information needs for and uses of online health-related information
  • Innovative applications of simulation, user customization, virtual environments or innovative information integration techniques to enhance or improve learning and retention in biomedical and health education

Example award: Improving access to multi-lingual health information through machine translation

Exploratory/Developmental Grants (R21) for Biomedical Informatics and Bioinformatics are also offered in support of early stage conceptual work and feasibility tests in biomedical informatics. These studies may involve considerable risk but may lead to a breakthrough in a particular area, or to the development of novel techniques or approaches that could have major impact on a field of biomedical, behavioral, or clinical research. Preliminary data are not required, but applicants are urged to provide milestones by which progress on the project can be measured. Topics of interest include the same themes described above for R01 projects.

Example award: eHealth Literacy Barriers and Solutions for Consumer Use of a Health Portal

NLM also participates in a number of NIH multi-Institute research initiatives. Among such are:

  • Understanding and Promoting Health Literacy Grants (R01 and R21) Support to encourage empirical research on health literacy concepts, theory and interventions
  • Behavioral and Social Science Research on Understanding and Reducing Health Disparities Grants (R01 and R21) Support for informatics-centric projects relevant to behavioral and social science research on the causes and solutions to health and disability disparities in the U.S. population
  • NIH Basic Behavioral and Social Sciences Opportunity Network (OppNET) Grants NLM's interests focus on novel informatics approaches needed to capture, represent, organize, analyze or integrate research findings related to social and behavioral science priorities of OppNet

Resource grants differ from research grants, and are designed to improve the dissemination, management and use of biomedical knowledge. These applications are judged during review by criteria other than those used for research applications. Applications must address the theme(s) selected and described by NLM in the annual announcement, which in 2010 offered support for projects to bring useful health information to health disparity populations and their health care providers.

Example award: A Multiethnic Digital Corpus of Speech for Older Persons

NLM supports training of informaticians to assure an adequate national pool of informatics researchers and health information specialists. Training is offered at eighteen NLM programs across the nation, and through individual fellowships. Approximately half of the trainees supported by NLM pursue careers in areas relevant to consumer health informatics.
Conference Support (R13) is available for scientific meetings, conferences, and workshops that are relevant to scientific development in biomedical informatics, bioinformatics, computational biology, and information sciences. If sufficiently open and of general use, conferences relevant to consumer health informatics are eligible to apply.
Small businesses with business ideas grounded in informatics can apply to NLM's Small Business Innovation Research (SBIR) and Small Business Technology Transfer Research (STTR) grants programs. Recent areas of interest to NLM have included the Semantic Web, and Intelligent Search Agents, but any topic, including consumer health, relevant to the NLM mission is suitable for these grants.

Example Award: Bayesian Textual and Multimedia Information Retrieval

NLM has no control over the federal budget to dictate how much funding is available from year to year, but clearly, informatics is at the heart of our grant programs; awards relevant to consumer health constitute a substantial portion of our portfolio. Decades of support for electronic health records exemplify NLM's interest in applying information technology to support both those who provide care and those who receive care. Current problems of particular interest include: research, development and application of appropriate decision support needed to exploit efficiently the current federal push to implement electronic records widely; better understanding of how to influence behavior with information; and improving accessibility of the public to health information of high quality in a cultural climate in which information is easily available but often anecdotal, commercially motivated, or otherwise of dubious authenticity.

Detailed information about NLM's grant programs can be found at https://www.nlm.nih.gov/ep/. The site can be especially useful for those not familiar with NLM programs or NIH procedures because it includes contact information for NLM program officers who can answer questions and guide applicants. The site provides, as well, a list of awards in each grant category, a useful resource for those uncertain about the type of projects NLM will fund.

Fear and Loathing Across the Data Chasm

Posted on 03-09-2011
Erik M. Augustson, Ph.D. M.P.H.
Behavioral Scientist, Clinical Psychologist
Tobacco Control Research Branch
Behavioral Research Program
National Cancer Institute
National Institutes of Health

Recently, members from the Behavioral Research Program at the National Cancer Institute (NCI) presented a workshop at the annual conference for the Society for Research on Nicotine and Tobacco entitled "Behavioral Informatics for Tobacco Control: Opportunities and Challenges for Research, Policy, and Communication of Data." The presentations largely focused on health-oriented behavioral and social science in the era of "Big Data." That is, an era when easily accessible data can be measured in petabytes (one quadrillion bytes, 1000 terabytes, one million gigabytes), an amount of data that is truly impossible for most of us to grasp. A key question within the field of health and health research is how do we meaningfully shift though and integrate this vast amount of data such that we can identify unexpected patterns and findings that allow us to better inform our science and improve health?

Within this context, two innovative NCI initiatives were used to highlight key points. The Population Science Grid Community Health Data Portal (PopSciGrid) is an evolving proof-of-concept for communication and harmonization of health-related data for consumers and policy-makers. The goal of this project is to visualize behavioral and other types of publicly available data using analytic tools and graphics for community impact. Closely linked to this effort is the Grid Enabled Measures (GEM) project. This project has created a platform that is designed to facilitate the development of an online community of scientists to collaborate on two goals: 1) identify, vet, and promote the use of standardized behavioral and social science measures, especially those which are based on theoretically-meaningful constructs, and 2) use these standardized measures to assist in sharing and harmonization of data. Although the workshop presentations focused on these two initiatives, these ultimately served as jumping off points for the discussion of much broader themes, which I would like to take a few moments to consider.

As a general comment, I was struck by how fundamental concerns about the changing landscape of data continue to be unresolved for many of the workshop participants. Of course the "sample" was skewed toward people who were not familiar with topics associated with Big Data and the growing movement toward the democratization of data, otherwise why would you attend the workshop? Still, my sense is that there is a growing tension within behavioral and social science communities (and probably beyond) that is not being acknowledged, let alone addressed. On the one side, there are the health information technology (HIT) techies who are fully embracing the use of emerging technologies to better understand health issues and are forging ahead as fast as budgets and technology limitations will allow. The enthusiasm and energy of these folks practically crackles in the air around them. On the other side of this widening data chasm is a large, and I would suggest often forgotten, contingent of health researchers and administrators. The downside of the growing enthusiasm for health informatics is that the concerns (and by that I mean fears) of the people who create, collect, and hold most of the data are not being addressed to the extent that they should.

Although for different reasons, both these groups have long-standing histories of fiercely protecting data access, maintaining tight control over assessment instruments, and a predilection for developing scales relevant to their own work and projects. If we step back and consider the contextual forces that helped to shape this approach to the development of assessment instruments, data collection and management, and indeed research itself, (e.g., publish or perish, IRB concerns, HIPPA and other privacy issues, limited access to others’ assessment instruments, a general complete lack of access to others’ data) this world view makes a great deal of sense. We need to remember that these are professionals who have achieved, or are desperately trying to achieve, success in their careers by carefully crafting arguments that support a line of research based on the thoughtful consideration of what their data "means." They immerse themselves in the data and protect against people who may misunderstand this meaning. This can be the case of data collected within a single lab using freshmen psychology students, a state public health project, or a national initiative. My experience has been that discussions about data sharing and harmonization universally elicit fear among these folks.

I recognize that this is a smidge overstated, but I encourage those of us in HIT, especially those interested in large scale efforts to facilitate data sharing and the democratization of data, to pause and consider where the "owners" of much of this data are coming from, and to recognize that their professional reality needs to be taken into consideration.

I must confess that I have been guilty of not really listening to what these people are saying. Mostly I have rolled my eyes (at least internally) and made some sort of dismissive blanket statement about how the gates have already been opened and our data is no longer ours. After now having made this type of statement to a variety of groups several times, it has finally dawned on me that my doing so is a disservice to both those on the other side of the chasm and in the open data camp.

I want to suggest that concerted efforts need to be made to educate and engage those on the other side of the data chasm. We need to spend more time cultivating relationships with those not on the bus, singing in the choir, who have not drunk the Kool-Aid, or whatever your favorite metaphor may be.

So given this, let me spend my last few minutes discussing what I perceive as the main concern: misuse of the data. This takes a few forms. First, that someone, typically the general public, will misunderstand the data and draw false conclusions that are then propagated into the universe. Second, people will analyze the data differently, whether on purpose or due to a lack of knowledge regarding the data structure, and then "publish" the results in some form outside of the peer review process. Third, the data will be used by some entity to promote an agenda that in turn hurts public health efforts. (This latter concern is the one that seems to draw the most intense anxiety, especially when the data is health-related.) This might take the form of discrediting existing health programs, questioning programs’ value and impact, and/or denying that the data supports the link between a health outcome and a given exposure or behavior. These fears are especially evident in contexts where program funding is in jeopardy or there are large and litigious stakeholders (e.g., an industry whose product has been linked to a negative health outcome).

These fears are legitimate but at the same time, the open data movement is rolling and cannot be turned back. Not addressing these fears may serve to hinder efforts to move forward. Thus a major challenge we must directly address in our efforts to bridge the data chasm is how can these issues be dealt with given that those who have historically been able to tightly control their data will not be able to effectively do so for much longer? It is my hope that those active in the democratization of data will work with the gatekeepers and owners of data to tackle this head-on. Rather than proclaiming that if they want to run with the big dogs then they need to leave the puppies on the porch, I suggest that the answers lie in cooperation on developing methods of structuring data and providing user friendly data management/analysis tools to reduce misunderstandings by lay users and truly provide a means for the data to reach its potential to inform and guide us in our efforts to improve health at local, state, national, and international levels.

Insights in Funding: National Science Foundation

An Introduction to NSF

The National Science Foundation (NSF), an independent federal agency, was created by Congress in 1950. With an annual budget of about $6.9 billion (FY 2010), NSF is the funding source for approximately 20 percent of all federally supported basic research conducted by America's colleges and universities, plus some non-profit organizations. In fields such as mathematics, computer science, and the social and behavioral sciences, NSF is the major source of federal funding.

The NSF is divided into the following seven directorates that support science and engineering research and education: Biological Sciences (BIO); Computer and Information Science and Engineering (CISE); Geosciences (GEO); Mathematics and Physical Sciences (MPS); Social, Behavioral and Economic Sciences (SBE); Engineering (ENG); and Education and Human Resources (EHR). Each directorate is further subdivided into divisions, and typically, divisions are further divided into programs.

NSF uses two general review criteria for all proposals: Intellectual Merit and Broader Impacts. Under intellectual merit, reviewers are asked to comment on:

  • How important is the proposed activity to advancing knowledge and understanding within its own field or across different fields?
  • How well qualified is the proposer (individual or team) to conduct the project? (If appropriate, the reviewer will comment on the quality of prior work.)
  • To what extent does the proposed activity suggest and explore creative, original, or potentially transformative concepts?
  • How well conceived and organized is the proposed activity?
  • Is there sufficient access to resources?

For broader impacts, reviewers are asked to address:

  • How well does the activity advance discovery and understanding while promoting teaching, training, and learning?
  • How well does the proposed activity broaden the participation of underrepresented groups (e.g., gender, ethnicity, disability, geographic, etc.)?
  • To what extent will it enhance the infrastructure for research and education, such as facilities, instrumentation, networks, and partnerships?
  • Will the results be disseminated broadly to enhance scientific and technological understanding?
  • What may be the benefits of the proposed activity to society?

Prospective investigators should consult both a program's solicitation (advertised on the web pages of various divisions and programs) and NSF's Proposal and Award Policies and Procedures Guide when preparing proposals; they can also consult with NSF Program Directors in the appropriate programs. NSF's web site—www.nsf.gov—has a lot of information and is relatively easy to navigate.

Support of Health-related Research

NSF funds a significant amount of health-related research. Such funding opportunities are spread throughout the Foundation, and are sometimes embedded within programs in various fields that do NOT mention the word "health" in their title. This is important to realize when perusing NSF solicitations. For example, one of the Foundation-wide multi-disciplinary programs that can accommodate health-related research is the Cyber-enabled Discovery and Innovation (CDI) program. The CDI solicitation does not explicitly mention health. Instead, the solicitation notes, "Collectively, CDI research outcomes are expected to produce paradigm shifts in our understanding of a wide range of science and engineering phenomena and socio-technical innovations that create new wealth and enhance the national quality of life." Yet, "national quality of life" clearly includes health-related research, and consumer health informatics would be a part of that. It is important that people searching for funding opportunities at NSF read the program solicitations carefully and in detail to determine whether their project will fit into the program.

In addition to special initiatives, many long-standing, established programs throughout NSF also fund research that simultaneously fosters innovation and basic research in a particular field and advances national health.
A few examples of current NSF projects* that address health-related research include:

  • The Center for Eldercare and Rehabilitation Technology, University of Missouri, Columbia is an example of NSF support for cyber-enabled team science. It is funded under a joint program by Engineering and Computer Science called Cyber Physical Systems (CPS). The Center has six active projects, which are funded by the NSF, NIH, and other funding agencies. One of the projects: "Active Heterogeneous Sensing for Fall Detection and Fall Risk Assessment" (funded under the CPS award #0931607) investigates adaptive sensor fusion and intelligent decision making technologies to monitor and detect when older adults fall in their home environment.
  • A recent NSF award (CDI award #1028076) uses machine learning and pattern recognition to analyze children's behavioral cues to try to predict which children will develop psychiatric disorders (or not). This project builds on behavioral science findings that demonstrate how psychiatric disorders are culminated by behaviors that can be observed, learned, and correlated. It uses modern computational tools to develop new methods of diagnosis and intervention.
  • Another example is the project funded from the Social, Behavioral and Economic Sciences (SBE) Directorate, from the Division of Social and Economic Sciences (SES), entitled "Legal and Insurance Environments Impact and Health Practices (SES award #0958190). This research examines the effects of medical malpractice suits, tort laws, health insurance, and reproductive regulations on the rates of cesarean sections in the United States.

A New Initiative: Smart Health and Wellbeing

In 2010, the CISE Directorate announced a program named Smart Health and Wellbeing (SHW). The goal of the Smart Health and Wellbeing program is to seek improvements in safe, effective, efficient, equitable, and patient-centered health and wellness services through innovations in computer and information science and engineering. Doing so requires leveraging the scientific methods and knowledge bases of a broad range of computing and communication research perspectives. NSF also has a Working Group dedicated to Smart Health, which meets regularly to coordinate and foster activities at the Foundation level.

As one effort to discuss international efforts and analyze new directions in health informatics, the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development [OECD] and NSF organized a workshop, held in February of this year. The workshop, called "Building a Smarter Health and Wellness Future," highlighted how different countries are using smart health to support aging populations, develop electronic patient records, use mobile devices to gather research data, and the like. More information, including a link to the OECD-NSF workshop agenda, can be found at: http://www.oecd.org/internet/ieconomy/oecd-nsfworkshopbuildingasmarterhealthandwellnessfuture.htm. A detailed summary of the workshop is available at the Computing Community Consortium's blog.

NSF has many different avenues to accommodate scientific research for healthcare and related issues. There is a wealth of opportunities that directly relate to innovation in informatics for consumer health. Interested persons should explore the agency's website to find appropriate programs and NSF staff contact information.

* More information on these awards, including abstracts, names of Principal Investigators and their institutions are available on NSF's Award Search page found at www.nsf.gov/awardsearch/. Typing the award number in the field "Search Award For" yields the links to the information. Interested researchers may find the information useful for various purposes.

Provider-Centric Data Exchange or Patient-Centric Care--When will we get to Meaningful Impact?

Posted on 03-24-2011
Susan Nedza MD, MBA FACEP
Vice President, Strategic Clinical Solutions
HealthyCircles™; LLC

The difference between meaningful use and meaningful impact became very clear to me during the completion of a Robert Wood Johnson Foundation funded-research study on Primary Care Physicians, HIT and Quality Improvement Activities. The Chief Medical Officer of a large physician practice summed up the bottom line in a few words, "None of this will matter, if it doesn't help us take better care of patients." That was it. He challenged policy makers and technology vendors to keep focused on what matters most—creating incentives that spur the development of innovative technologies that enhance the ability of professionals to provide patient-centric care.
Unfortunately, our focus has often been provider-centric and only focused on data-exchange between the traditional hospital and physician-practice setting. There has yet to emerge a consensus response to two fundamental questions: "Where is the patient in all of this?" and "How can we improve outcomes unless all professionals who actively participate in care are connected?". We know that patients and families see the value of sharing information as well. A recent survey by the Markle Foundation revealed that roughly 80 percent of both the public and doctors agree that it is important to require participating hospitals and doctors to share information to better coordinate care, cut unnecessary costs, and reduce medical errors. So, where is the common ground? It resides in the 2015 goals for the meaningful use of Health Information Technology:

  • Engage Patients and Families
  • Improve Care Coordination
  • Improve quality, safety, efficiency and reduce health disparities
  • Ensure adequate privacy and security protections for personal health information
  • Interoperability with health information systems

So, the path forward is clear. Health information technology must support collaboration across the technological, organizational and geographic boundaries that separate healthcare professionals, patients and families. The endeavor will only be successful when every individual who contributes to the care team is included, and when patients and family members are equal participants in information exchange. So, how is this being accomplished?

In the public sector, the Health Resources and Services Administration (HRSA) sponsored Patient Safety and Clinical Pharmacy Collaborative has successfully shown the value of integrating evidence-based clinical pharmacy services into the care and management of high-risk, high-cost, complex patients in over 128 sites that span 43 states. By including clinical pharmacists on the team and focusing on sharing pharmacy data at the point of care, they have successfully improved quality, optimized efficacy and implemented a model for minimize the risk of adverse events. Efforts are currently underway to strengthen the infrastructure that will enable this model to become sustainable and scalable.

In the private sector, organizations are using integrated health solutions such as the HealthyCircles Connected Care Platform to accomplish the virtual integration of pharmacy services into the care delivery process. This virtual integration of services is a cost-effective strategy as it provides access to the record sharing information system that connects patients, families, and health care professionals through secure social networking technology.

For example, a subset of patients undergoing treatment for specific conditions that require specialty pharmacy services (e.g., oncology, home-infusion) are registered on the HealthyCircles platform and receive access to a robust personal health record. The patients are encouraged to invite a family caregiver(s) to participate actively in their care. At the same time, prescribing professionals are invited to access the shared record and to utilize the clinical tools available via the professional portal. This connectivity simplifies the administrative processes around procurement of these medications and frees the patient and their family from needless complexity. On the clinical side, physicians can track their patients' progress and modify treatment plans as necessary based upon the patient's feedback captured in daily health programs, and intermittent assessments. In addition, the prescribing physicians and other professionals can now coordinate care with the insurer and specialty pharmacy professionals as they participate jointly in medication review, share lab results, and message patients in a cost-effective manner. A connected health platform like HealthyCircles enables the organization to deliver evidence-driven care, to improve access and to drive cost-savings through more efficient care.

If Health Information Technology is to have a meaningful impact, only one question will define our success. Did it help us to take better care of more patients at a dramatically reduced cost? We know that information technology has effectively enabled significant cost savings and efficiencies across many industries. What remains to be seen is if the paradox of technology driving costs up in healthcare will be broken this time. Richard Foster, the Chief Actuary for the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services in his testimony before the House Budget Committee on January 27, 2011 when asked about the value of technology responded,
"If there's a way to turn around the mindset for the people who do the research and development … to get them to focus more on cost-reducing tech and less on cost increasing technology, if you can do that then one of biggest components of [increasing costs] turns to your side. Let's hope that the meaningful use standards accomplish this focus."

Let's not just hope, but commit, to developing and adopting technology that has a meaningful impact on the quality of care we deliver.

Methodological Considerations in Analyzing Social Media Data

Posted on 05-02-2011
Karley Abramson, M.P.H.
Cancer Research Training Award Fellow
Wen-ying Sylvia Chou, Ph.D., M.P.H.
Program Director
Health Communication and Informatics Research Branch
National Cancer Institute, National Institutes of Health

One of our current priorities in the National Cancer Institute's Health Communication and Informatics Research Branch (HCIRB) is to understand the ways social media are used for cancer prevention and control in order to inform effective programs and interventions. This effort requires deeper insight into the naturally-occurring activity that takes place on social media sites. In a project researching Facebook interactions about breast cancer, we encountered many methodological decision points that we'd like to discuss.
We chose to focus on Facebook pages concerning breast cancer awareness because of its high visibility in the media (including social media), and also because it combines our interests: Sylvia has substantial quantitative and qualitative research experience in social media for health communication, while Karley, a "Facebook native", brings multiple perspectives as a public health researcher and a Facebook participant. Our project attempts to answer the following questions:

  • How are non-profit organizations using Facebook pages to promote breast cancer awareness, and what kinds of content are they posting?
  • How do users interact with each other and with the organization? Where on the page do people 'gather' and talk?
  • What are the main functions of Facebook posts from users and the organizations hosting the pages?

We encountered methodological questions from the onset. In addition to the massive amount of information being exchanged, Facebook interactions are highly unstructured and minimally moderated, posing significant challenges to coding research. The variation in user intent and participation creates an open space suitable for an endless range of communication and interaction.

Methodological challenges and our decisions:

Defining our data

Our first task was to narrow down the available content into relevant and analyzable data. We considered:

  • The number of pages/organizations to include.
  • The depth and detail ofanalysis on each page.
  • Which areas or sections of the page to analyze (Wall, Discussion board, Pictures, etc.).
  • The time-frame.
  • Criteria for determining the importance of the pages (notability, popularity, intentions/goals of pages, etc.).

After consideration of the abundance of pages dedicated to breast cancer, we decided to focus our analysis to a case-study of one page and examine the content of the Wall posts during October of 2010 (Breast Cancer Awareness Month), the month of the year when the sites are most active. We chose one particular page (Breast Cancer Awareness ) based on membership totals, indicated by the number of "likes" or "fans" for each page, and the stated intentions of the organization for awareness.

To code, or not to code?

Our next obstacle was to decide how to best describe and analyze the data. We encountered numerous challenges:

  • Overlapping functions of posts: many comments and responses serve multiple purposes.
  • Difficulties in disentangling "push" messages from "pull" messages.
  • Inexhaustible range of topics that extend beyond breast cancer.
  • Unpredictable patterns of conversation and user exchange.

As we became more engaged in this research, we realized that traditional methods of qualitative research couldn't adequately capture the multi-dimensional interactions that occur in social media formats. We would need to devise an innovative way to analyze, organize, and present the data without subjecting it to the restrictions of a traditional coding analysis.

Going with the (data) flow

To say the least, we are hardly starved for data, and as expected, we must address the difficulty in creating a conceptual framework that can adequately account for all of the activity and interaction that occurs on these pages. The democratic user participation creates content that is layered with multiple meanings, connotations, and intentions, and we find ourselves ideating "big picture" findings and restructuring them into more concrete, codable results. This study suggests to us that when the goal is to describe Facebook content and user activities, a bottom-up approach is more appropriate. For instance, we have found that organization-generated posts often serve multiple purposes that extend beyond their original intentions. In one example, Breast Cancer Awareness (BCA ) posts a message about a new product (Pink Ribbon pants) and encourages users to make purchases to help fund mammograms for women in need. Users are then free to respond in any manner they choose. In this instance, users take over the space and begin a conversation about where to find affordable mammograms. Another user exploits this same space to promote their products (to support ovarian cancer research). This example demonstrates the unsystematic nature of Facebook interactions, and illustrates how conversations can occur in unpredictable locations.

What's Next?

Developing research methodologies needed for this form of media will require creativity, flexibility, and a willingness to re-conceptualize the meaning of health communication. Once we have developed and advance our understanding of social media as a communication phenomenon, social media-based health interventions will be more informed and therefore more effective. Please stay tuned for the results of our analysis, which we are hoping to share through presentations and a publication in the near future.
We also welcome comments and discussions. How are you optimizing research methods to capture and understand social media data to improve consumer health?

Where Does Informatics Fit In: A Transdisciplinary Approach

Paul K. Courtney
SAIC, Inc. NCI-Frederick
Biomedical Informatics Coordinator
Office of the Director, Division of Cancer Control and Population Science
National Cancer Institute

I recently had the pleasure of attending a meeting where I heard about systems-oriented approaches to cancer research, the American Society of Preventive Oncology Meeting (ASPO) 2011 Annual Meeting. A systems-approach is based on the concept of the human body being composed of “interdependent elements interacting to achieve a common aim.” The most important common aim is to continue living, of course, but other smaller systems are responsible for keeping our bodies appropriately supplied with oxygen with carbon dioxide removed – this is the cardiopulmonary system. The presentations covered areas like tobacco, obesity, diet, environmental exposures and how team science approaches to transdisciplinary research could be used to investigate the many dimensions of cancer prevention. At the National Cancer Institute, I have had the honor to work with a diverse group of researchers on a range of biomedical informatics projects across the cancer care continuum in a collaborative atmosphere. I noticed this same atmosphere of “collaboration” at ASPO’s annual meeting, and as an informatician, I began to wonder about what kind of informatics infrastructure we need to support these transdisciplinary approaches to cancer research.

There is a prevalence and preponderance of simple, linear models of health that we all use in our everyday lives. For some types of unwellness this is appropriate and useful. If we have a cold, we take more vitamin C, we rest, we drink more fluids and so on. We can do just one or do all of these things but we don't expect the effect of more rest to somehow multiply the effects of more fluids, just that doing both is better than just one. However for many other kinds of health challenges the reality is messier.
Messier because we are all different due to our genetic inheritance, habits of smoking, diet and exercise and family health history. More interesting, because there is research into all of these factors and their interactions that is starting to tease out the basis for choosing the kinds of therapies that are appropriate for you.

A good example is the challenge of helping someone to quit smoking. You may know someone who, after smoking for ten or twenty years, was able to just decide to stop “cold turkey”; not that it was without difficulty but there seemed to be no agony involved in the process. You also no doubt know others who quit smoking and relapsed several times and some who just gave up after a while. A simple model of this would be that it is merely a matter of who has the most will power, but this oversimplifies the issues involved. The research is showing that the reasons for differences in the ability to stop smoking are linked to our genetic predisposition and to the ways that nicotine can hijack our brains.

At the ASPO meeting, Dr. Peter Shields from Georgetown University presented a review of studies entitled, “Where Do We Stand on Genetic Predictors of Smoking Behaviors?”, representing a number of different research domains: neuroscience, genetics, GWAS, pharmacogenetics and behavioral medicine. Using a transdisciplinary approach can be challenging since the language and concepts of each domain are so different, but Dr. Shields was able to show how the evidence from one domain could be linked with that from another, eventually connecting a person’s inherited genetic code to the drug that is best in helping that person to stop smoking.
Briefly, the nicotine molecule is able to breeze through the blood-brain barrier and act as a neurotransmitter in the opioid/dopamine circuitry. In this way, nicotine acts similarly to other drugs such as cocaine and heroin. The nicotine actually can reprogram the neurally-based response of smokers – although not all smokers – to visual stimuli related to smoking, something that can be detected looking at something called ERPs (Event Related brain Potentials) using high-density EEG. This reprogramming has the effect of making it much harder for a person to stop smoking. Since not everyone has the same difficulty quitting and not everyone responds the same to different drugs to help quit, the question arises is it possible to personalize the therapy using our genetic profile? Could a relatively simple EEG test be used to indirectly identify that genetic profile?

Dr. Shields showed that research has demonstrated a relationship between our genes and our ability to quit smoking with the assistance of two different drugs, buproprion and varenicline; some of us do better with one or the other and our genetic profile helps to decide which one to choose. Now that is personalized medicine!

After I left this presentation I reflected on how there are two challenges to informatics implicit in this research. The first is the ability to aggregate and integrate data from many different domains and levels – genetic to behavioral – and across heterogeneous data sources with varying terminologies and taxonomies. The second is a result of the cross- and transdisciplinary nature of this work; it requires individuals who are willing to engage in collaboration and teamwork. An informatics infrastructure that can support transdisciplinary research is emerging from efforts made in Semantic Web technologies (RDF, OWL and ontologies). This framework enables the meaningful connections of disparate data sources with the use of ontologies; it can then provide different researchers with a view of the underlying data that is meaningful to them. This concept can then be used to access the same data in order to support evidence-based communication of information that is meaningful to consumers.

Many public and private organizations have moved towards more consumer-focused, personalized medicine. However, what happens if we don’t have the proper infrastructure in place to support this? And, can we get there faster if that infrastructure supports team science approaches to transdisciplinary research?

Patient-facing Health IT: Bridging or Widening the Digital Divide?

Posted on 06-21-2011
Urmimala Sarkar, MD, M.P.H.
Assistant Professor
University of California San Francisco
San Francisco General Hospital Center for Vulnerable Populations

With all the recent discussion of internet use, health information technology (HIT) and health care, it’s important to talk about who might be getting left behind. With the HIT revolution, the digital divide is becoming an increasingly important consideration for health care. As a primary care provider in a safety-net health system, I worry that my patients, who are low-income and often uninsured, may not benefit from the widespread implementation of patient-facing health information technology because they lack access or ability to use the internet.

This concern led my collaborators and me to examine use of a leading patient portal, Kaiser Permanente’s state-of-the-art secure portal (https://healthy.kaiserpermanente.org/ ). We looked at more than 14,000 English-speaking adults with diabetes who are part of the Diabetes Study of Northern California (DISTANCE) -- making it the largest observational study to assess use of a patient portal among diabetes patients in the United States (available online at Journal of the American Medical Informatics Associationhttp://jamia.oxfordjournals.org/ and Journal of Health Communication http://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/10810730.2010.499988 ). We chose patients with diabetes because we know they have significant health needs and expected them to make use of a patient portal.

Internet-based patient portals allow patients to access their health information, including their diagnostic test results, to perform health-related tasks such as making medical appointments and requesting refills of prescription medications, and to interact with providers through secure email messages. Such patient portals are considered a core component of “meaningful use” of health information technology under federal health reform legislation, and will be rapidly implemented across U.S. health care settings in the coming years. If low-income populations, ethnic minority groups, and those with limited educational attainment and health literacy have less access to or familiarity with this technology, there is good reason to worry that they will fall further behind in terms of their health. This fits with the “inverse care law,” the observation that health innovations can actually widen health disparities as they disproportionately benefit advantaged segments of the population.

In our study, we found that people with limited education, in certain racial/ ethnic minority groups (African-American and Latino/a), and those with limited health literacy, are less likely to sign up to use an internet- based patient portal. Even among those that did sign up via the internet for the patient portal, Latino/a populations and African Americans, as well as those with limited health literacy, were less likely to actually use it. This is particularly worrisome because the patients less likely to use the patient portal are those who are already known to have worse diabetes outcomes.

Our results suggest that a digital divide exists on multiple distinct levels. First, there is a well-known lack of access to computers and the internet among low-income and ethnic minority populations. Lack of access and interest likely explains our finding of less people signing up to use the patient portal. Overcoming this obstacle by strengthening internet connectivity and computer access across all U.S. communities is critical to addressing the digital divide in health. Specific outreach targeted to these groups about patient portal availability and use may also bridge this gap. Beyond access, however, is the issue of navigation of patient-directed health information technology. I suspect that some people who signed up for the patient portal, but did not use it, had difficulty using the actual patient portal site. However, I would not be surprised if many people who have difficulty successfully using an internet-based patient portal are able to use the internet for other purposes. Building in features such as access in multiple languages, layout and language appropriate for those with limited health literacy, and smart design to help with navigation, should be fundamental to all patient-facing health information technology, not an afterthought.

The internet has tremendous potential to use video, images, and multilingual techniques, with wide population reach. This could greatly enhance comprehensibility of health care and health promotion. Patient portals additionally can expand access to core health activities such as managing prescription medications and communicating with health care providers. If we want to realize this potential to improve public health, we need to not only increase access, but also improve usability and lower the literacy demands of patient-facing HIT. It’s time to move beyond the inverse care law and bring all populations into the digital age for health.

The ‘Science of Team Science’: Best Practices for Team-Based Collaboration

Posted on 06-23-2011
Amanda L. Vogel, Ph.D., MHS and Kara L. Hall, Ph.D.

If you are working on projects to enhance behavioral support for healthcare consumers, you may be collaborating with a diverse set of partners such as physicians, applied health professionals, health administrators, informatics and technology experts, patients and their families, and other stakeholders.

Collaborations that cast a wide net in terms of their membership may benefit greatly from the variety of perspectives, skills, and expertise that team members bring to bear. For example they can create innovations by applying or combining approaches from a wide variety of disciplines, professions, or fields. In fact teams with diverse membership are increasingly encouraged and supported, as a means of stimulating innovation and creativity.

But collaborations with diverse members must also address some unique challenges that are introduced by this approach, if they wish to maximize their effectiveness. For example once you compose a team of diverse members, it is important to engage in a deliberate group process to develop joint goals that are also relevant to each team member, shared understandings and expectations of the work process, and a shared vocabulary for discussing work processes and goals. Another challenge that needs to be addressed in order to allow members to contribute effectively and fully is how best to coordinate and integrate members’ activities given their unique areas of expertise, goals for the collaboration, and competing priorities.

Team-based collaborations that take place across geographic distances and institutions are increasingly common. These collaborations introduce unique benefits, foremost the ability to engage partners with specific expertise wherever they are located in the world. But they also introduce additional challenges, including how best to work with collaborators from institutions with very different organizational cultures, how to work effectively across time zones, or how to develop mutual understanding with collaborations from different cultural backgrounds who may have different communication styles and expectations for collaborations.

Luckily there is an entire field of scholarship and practice that is dedicated to identifying the best ways to address these challenges, and maximize the efficiency and effectiveness of collaborative science initiatives.

The “Science of Team Science” Field

The Science of Team Science (SciTS) field is a new and rapidly growing interdisciplinary field that is generating practical knowledge about how to maximize the effectiveness of team-based collaborations, as applied, specifically, to the conduct of research.

The SciTS field was created in response to the growing prevalence of cross-disciplinary and cross-field collaborative research initiatives that aim to address complex and multi-causal real-world problems, such as climate change and the growing prevalence of chronic disease.

SciTS scholars study the antecedent conditions and collaboration processes that influence the effectiveness of team science. The immediate aim of the field is to produce transferrable lessons about how to address challenges, maximize benefits, and ultimately improve the effectiveness of team-based science. The ultimate goal of the field is to enhance the scientific outcomes of collaborative, team-based research, including productivity and innovation.

The SciTS field is producing helpful practices for efficient and effective collaboration, which have applications to collaborations to develop and implement behavioral interventions. There are also many leaders in the U.S. and abroad who are creating platforms to disseminate best practices for collaborative team-based science. Two of these resources are the Team Science Toolkit, and the Annual International Science of Team Science Conference. The rest of this blog provides an introduction to these resources, including links where you go to learn more.

Finding Resources to Support Your Team-based Collaborations: The Team Science Toolkit—www.teamsciencetoolkit.cancer.gov

The National Cancer Institute’s SciTS Team has developed the Team Science Toolkit, a powerful online tool to help disseminate resources that can help maximize the efficiency and effectiveness of team science.

The Team Science Toolkit is an interactive website built on a wiki platform that enables users to share resources and information to support team approaches to research. Users can freely upload and download resources, provide comments about their experiences using a resource, and engage in online dialogue.

The Toolkit, which debuted in a beta version in April 2011, currently includes over 300 resources, and this number will continue to grow as users contribute to the site. Examples of resources currently in the Toolkit include:

  • Guidelines for developing “prenuptial agreements” among collaborators that address issues such as coauthorship, data sharing, and ownership over products of collaborations;
  • Self-assessment tools that teams can use to assess team processes in order to identify strengths and challenges, and tailored strategies to address these challenges;
  • Training materials and syllabi to train team members and students in skills for team science, measures that can be used to assess the processes and outcomes of team science;
  • Measures and instruments to study team science;
  • A comprehensive list of funding mechanisms that support collaborative team science;
  • Links to web-based platforms for research collaboration;
  • Promotion and tenure guidelines to recognize faculty who are engaged in team science; and
  • Bibliographic citations for over 200 articles and other references about team science, along with PDF files for many of these resources.

The Toolkit also offers a number of ways to connect with colleagues, including a blog by team science experts with related online discussion; a user-generated directory of team science experts who are willing to be contacted by colleagues; a link to register for the SciTS listserv co-sponsored by the NCI SciTS Team and the NUCATS Institute at Northwestern University; and user-generated bulletin boards that include postings about news, events, funding, and job opportunities.

The Potential of Multilevel Interventions to Improve Health IT

Posted on 07-11-2011
Heather Edwards, Ph.D., M.P.H.
SAIC-Frederick, Inc., Process of Care Research Branch, Division of Cancer Control and Population Science,
National Cancer Institute
Steve Taplin, MD, M.P.H.
Process of Care Research Branch, Division of Cancer Control and Population Science,
National Cancer Institute

The statistics on the U.S. health care system are often cited and lamented. From the floors of Congress to offices of local health clinics, the concerns over the health care system’s staggering costs and relatively poor return on investment compared to other countries command attention and prompt discussions about potential solutions.

A long held view of health behavior and health care delivery is that both are influenced by multiple levels of the environmental context. For example, people seeking care are affected by the care provider team who in turn are affected by the organization where they practice and the community wherein that organization operates, and all are affected by national health policy. Recently at national conference on Multilevel Interventions in Health Care, Stephen Taplin, MD, M.P.H., of the National Cancer Institute (NCI) explained that the past approach of focusing on one level hasn’t corrected the problems in health care delivery in the US, possibly because such approaches have not sufficiently considered the larger context in which care is delivered. Thus the Multilevel Interventions (MLI) project was created to foster ideas about improving care through interventions that target multiple levels and take into consideration how each level affects the others.

The NCI hosted the conference in March, 2011, in Las Vegas, to talk about this concept. Approximately 170 health professionals and researchers attended the conference and participated in discussions about how to improve health care by considering the complex, multilayered environment in which that care is delivered. Forming the foundation for discussion were 13 presentations covering the challenges and opportunities of multilevel research and application, keynote addresses by Drs. Richard Scott and Otis Brawley, and discussion sessions with conference participants. Videos of these sessions are posted on the National Institutes of Health’s YouTube channel. The papers presented at the conference will be published in an upcoming supplement to the Journal of the National Cancer Institute.

Informatics play an important role in multilevel interventions, both in terms of data tools for identifying targets or predicting effects of interventions (such as aggregated electronic health records data and intervention simulation applications) and health care improvement tools (such as decision support, care coordination programs, and health management apps). The process of implementing some health IT tools in health care delivery settings in order to achieve meaningful outcomes may require a multilevel approach.

For example, an electronic health records (EHR) system’s installation and effective use within a defined clinical setting will require support and buy-in at the organizational level (administrators) and the provider team level but also needs policy level support to facilitate adoption, as the HITECH Act proposes to do. In fact, adoption of electronic medical records has been slow until this point nationally. One might argue that the lack of alignment of all three levels has slowed implementation and that national policy that provides support and incentive is a critical third factor that must be aligned. The alternate scenarios are that a) without the financial or regulatory enticements to invest in EHR systems that are provided by policy, the costs of such investments would be prohibitive for many health care practices, b) without authorization from the administration, staff time and training for learning and using the system would be unsupported, and c) without provider team support/perceived utility for the records, one might predict a lack of utilization or incomplete utilization of the system. Should an intervention to increase and improve EHR use target only one level, resistance at another level could cause the EHRs to fail to reach their maximum benefit to patients and the health care system as a whole.

Clearly a simplification of the factors that contribute to EHR adoption, this scenario still illustrates the need for a multilevel approach to addressing health care delivery problems. Such an approach recognizes that although each level’s role may be distinctly defined, each interacts with and influences the other levels. Interventions that target multiple levels simultaneously will take into consideration the context of the other players in the decision and use of EHRs. While multilevel interventions in health IT have the potential to be as complicated as the problems they attempt to solve, the NCI multilevel team hopes the upcoming supplement to the Journal of the National Cancer Institute will provide useful guidance in the areas of design, measurement, and application of multilevel intervention research.

In the meantime, please check out the conference videos and join in the discussion about the challenges and opportunities of multilevel interventions in the field of health care informatics through this blog or by joining the LinkedIn Group “Multilevel Interventions in Health Care.” Specifically, we’d like to hear from you about your experiences with implementing health IT in clinical settings.

Improving Consumer Health IT Application Development: Lessons from Other Industries

Posted on 09-13-2011
Teresa Zayas Cabán, Ph.D.
Senior Manager, Health IT
Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality

The Institute of Medicine's (IOM), Crossing the Quality Chasm (2001) report lists the delivery of patient-centered care "that is respectful of and responsive to individual patient preferences, needs, and values" as one of the six critical components for transforming health care delivery. To realize this vision of a patient-centered health care system where "patient values guide all clinical decisions" (p. 40) we need information technologies and tools in place for consumers to store, retrieve, track, and share their health-related information and behaviors with their health care providers. Despite the best efforts of vocal industry advocates, consumers' adoption and use of those health information technologies and tools remains low. We believe enabling effective consumer adoption and use begins with successful product design.

Here at the Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality (AHRQ), we are working to ensure the effective design of consumer health information technology (IT) applications that improve health care quality through efforts such as our July 27–28, 2009 workshop, "Building Bridges: Consumer Needs and the Design of Health IT". One of the outgrowths of this successful workshop is our recently released report Improving Consumer Health IT Application Development: Lessons From Other Industries, Background Report (PDF). The report, authored by staff at the University of Maryland's Center for Health Information and Decision Systems at the Robert H. Smith School of Business (under a subcontract with Westat, Inc.), aptly summarizes and synthesizes the design methods used to develop successful consumer products in various non-health care related industries.

REVIEW OF COMMONLY USED DESIGN METHODS AND SUCCESSFUL PRODUCTS

The authors began by using Google™ and Google Scholar™ to conduct broad searches with keywords such as product development, consumer products, user-centered design, and product usability. They identified 18 distinct development methods which they characterized along seven dimensions:

  • Structure (low to high)
  • Iteration (low to high)
  • Span of approach (single stage to entire development cycle)
  • User involvement (low to high)
  • Design team composition (narrow to broad)
  • Novelty of product (low to high)
  • Virtualizability (low to high)

Next, the authors created a roadmap for understanding the design methods used in these non-health care related consumer products by selecting 24 popular products and classifying them as either:

  • Communication (including social networking)
  • e-Commerce
  • Information storage, archival, and retrieval;
  • Personalized entertainment;
  • Gaming;
  • Learning applications; or
  • Smart phone products.

The project team and subject matter experts determined consumer product popularity by estimating successful diffusion in recent years based on number of users and sales. Finally, several successful products from each of the product categories were identified and reviewed.

For each successful product, the team reviewed the category, name, type (purely digital product that may operate on a variety of platforms, or hybrid product incorporating an integrated physical device with a digital component as a single product), description, user characteristics, and design method(s) used. For each successful consumer product, the authors determined the design method(s) used and then identified the common design method themes across products. Again, they conducted Internet searches on Google® and Google Scholar™ using the product name in combination with keywords related to design (e.g., design, development, and user profile).

THE DESIGN METHODS AND CHARACTERISTICS OF SUCCESSFUL PRODUCTS

The top six most frequently used design methods were:

  • Prototyping
  • Agile development
  • Heuristic evaluation
  • Top-down design
  • Lean product development
  • Goals, Operations, Methods, and Selection Rules (GOMS)

The design teams' frequent use of these particular methods suggests that a focus on user experience with development methodologies incorporating iteration is increasing to respond to rapid changes in the market environment. In fact, the majority of the successful consumer products analyzed were developed using design methods with high levels of both user involvement and iteration. Additionally, organizations creating highly successful consumer products consistently use design methods that include high levels of iteration. This underscores that good design is an evolutionary process and that products themselves will be continually changing to respond to changing consumer preferences. The importance of prototyping in the iteration process is supported in the literature. Enabling consumers to use the product and provide feedback into the design process, rather than simply conducting interviews or focus groups, aids the determination of the functionality that users value in a product or service.

Also of note is that for the top three design methods used, the design team size and skill sets of all three were characterized as having medium breadth. This suggests that very large or highly diverse teams may detract from team effectiveness because of difficulties in communication and coordination.

In addition, many successful consumer products listed in the report were developed using more than one design method. However, hybrid products utilize multiple methods averaging almost 65 percent more design methods than purely digital products. By number, hybrid products use 4.4 methods compared to 2.6 methods for purely digital products. The overall average of design methods used for all products is 3.2.

The report also suggests that developers of successful consumer products approach design with an attention to the number of features incorporated into their products. This was evidenced through the review of the products studied, which were found to be streamlined and simple, at least at the user interface level, and not overloaded with features that overwhelm the user.

SUMMARY

To conclude, the findings for all 24 successful consumer products and their respective design methods are summarized below.
For all consumer products:

  • High levels of user involvement and iteration were used.
  • Product design teams usually utilized one or more of the following design methods: prototyping; agile development; heuristic evaluation; top-down design; lean product development; and GOMS.
  • Design teams typically had medium breadth in regard to team size and the skill sets represented.
  • Human factors experts were often engaged in the design team.
  • Designers selected a spare set of features in the application to "keep it simple." Careful attention was paid to user characteristics.

For hybrid consumer products:

  • Multiple design methods were typically used.
  • Designers generally used prototypes and consumer feedback based on their use of the prototype.

Our next activity for this project is to interview designers of successful consumer products. Their responses will contribute to a foundation of recommendations intended to guide the development of consumer health IT applications. This effort is one part of AHRQ's interest in better understanding and supporting individuals' personal health information management practices through the use of consumer health IT.

As part of this program of work, the AHRQ Health IT Portfolio published a related funding opportunity announcement, Understanding User Needs and Context to Inform Consumer Health Information Technology (IT) Design (R01). This new opportunity will fund projects supporting the development of a knowledge base about consumers' personal health information management needs and practices and related design principles. This opportunity is aligned with AHRQ's overarching interest in sponsoring research leading to a robust understanding of user needs and context which will impact consumer health IT design.

Making Health IT a Team Sport

Posted on 10-07-2011
Lindsey Hoggle
Managing Partner of Health Project Partners, LLC
Chair, AMIA's Consumer Health Informatics Working Group

On September 12, 2011, the Office of the National Coordinator of Health Information Technology, (ONC) released the Final Federal Health IT Strategic Plan (2011–2015) in response to feedback received on the draft version during the public comment period from March–May 2011. While this call for comments may not have attracted as much attention as other topics up for comment, this Plan is critical to the overall success of the HITECH Act's Electronic Health Record Incentive Program. As a nation, we need to "begin with the end in mind," so the draft Plan was the foundation for my previous ICH blog post.

It is important to appreciate that the Plan is a living document, informed by the 240 responses to the call for comments. It will be updated based on experiences, lessons learned, and the collaborative assimilation of strategy from a team from varied backgrounds. ONC's HealthIT Buzz Blog provided an overview of the main changes made to the Plan. There are five key topics which took on a stronger focus in the final version:

  • Barriers to adoption and health information exchange. What is standing in the way of our use of health IT?
  • Standards harmonization. In order to exchange digital health data, computers—and people—need to be able to speak the same language.
  • Coordination of care—or perhaps that is my interpretation of "support for the inclusion of providers not eligible for incentive payments under the Medicare and Medicaid EHR Incentive Programs in information exchange for purposes of care coordination."
  • Accessibility. Regardless of situations or disparities, we need to make health care—and good health—available to all.
  • Team health care. There is concern that the private sector—and other non-physician providers—are not engaged in learning about efforts [to improve the health care system.

Almost at the same time of the Final Plan release, President Obama declared September 11–17, 2011 National Health IT Week. It served as a transition week, where many events marked renewed commitment and, most importantly, a conviction that we can and will improve health care. In my mind, it was no coincidence that this transitional event coincided with the onset of the fall season, shifting from sweltering summer days to the crisp touch of autumn. Washington, D.C. is an area fortunate to have equal access to all the seasons; just as one grows weary of the heat of summer, the mornings suddenly become chilly—almost cold—and the leaves seem to fade overnight to brilliant fall hues. Around the same time, the nation turns to an infectious preoccupation with football. Be it college, the National Football League (NFL), or even the local high school, there is a nonstop attention to the details of the sport—well, at least for some of us! In my mind, the launch of football season and adoption of health IT have some very convenient similarities.

The Culture of Safety Must Be Pervasive

As with any sport, the safety of its players and fans is first and foremost. This year, the NFL added a layer of safety rules to the sport, causing grumbling among some fans. The point is—the NFL leaders are in constant motion on evaluating potential or existing risks to players. This "culture of safety" has to be a cornerstone to health IT adoption. Beginning with the Institute of Medicine report To Err is Human: Building a Safer Health System, the "safety" practices of health care across the country have come under the magnifying glass. It is not a pretty picture. A focus on quality and best practices for safety is now woven into almost every discussion on health care. And, most importantly, it is recognized as everybody's problem. Many initiatives have evolved from this discussion; the new National Priorities Partnership, for example, is a multi-stakeholder collaborative which serves to keep the focus on the right priorities and create a culture of safety and quality care practices.

Failed Handoffs Can Be a Show-Stopper

You don't have to be a football fan to appreciate the fact that any glitch in handing off the ball brings progress to a standstill. It can happen in a multitude of ways—be it a handoff from the quarterback, a short pass, or a "Hail Mary." There has to be a well-executed plan, including clear communication, split-second timing, and attention to detail. These details also pertain to health care. Not only do we have a fragmented system where personal health information is spread over a variety of different providers, but we have historically failed at the handoffs or transitions of care—so much so, that ONC this year established the Transitions of Care Initiative within its Standards and Interoperability Framework. This initiative serves to establish critical characteristics and data that are vital for a patient to be transferred successfully from one facility to another, to the home, or to caregivers. Just as in football, botching a handoff can have permanent, undesirable consequences.

Teamwork Is Key

This is my favorite area, yet one that needs considerably more focus. Football could not exist without the team concept. Take away a key player and the quality deteriorates. Communication is critical; everyone has a specific role and there has to be a coach. The defensive team has to work artfully with the offensive team or the results are dismal. Health care is no different. While the defense/offense mechanism appears to reflect conflicting processes within the game structure, it does not. The focus and intended outcome is the same—to win the game. Health care also has what appears to be opposing roles—providers, insurance plans, hospitals. Their goals, however are the same—to take the best possible care of the patient. There needs to be a "coach," or provider, who knows and utilizes the best available care for the patient—thus, the medical home model. All the team members have to understand their role, feel respected as a contributor, and work diligently to bring their best to the table. Payment mechanisms need to be in place. The patient has to be on the team. We are moving away from a culture of patients on the sidelines or in the stands, watching as the game is played. Patients have to be active team players for our health system to improve.

Change Is Certain

The past few years have been difficult not only because of the challenging economic conditions, but the realization that our health system is neither affordable nor very effective. Just as football teams change their strategies to field a winning team, adapt to the rules necessary to protect their players, and show football fans what they want to see, health care best practices have to evolve as evidence-based care and the team needs to work collaboratively to implement sound practices and treatments. Now is the time for the team huddle to improve health care through the use of health IT—and to understand what changes must take place. Perhaps this is the critical difference between football and health care: there is no reason we cannot all be winners.

Can We Close the Research-to-Practice Gap in Health Care?

Posted on 11-03-2011
Janey Sturtz McMillen, Ph.D.
Research Director
3-C Institute for Social Development

The National Institutes of Health (NIH) invests over $31.2 billion annually in medical research (NIH, 2011) much of it devoted to treatment studies which are vitally important. Unfortunately, the Institute of Medicine (IOM, 2001) reports it takes an average of 17 years for clinical research findings to be used regularly in clinical practice (p. 13). How do we "close the gap"—as numerous Federal reports have called us to do—to move effective interventions and practices from the strict conditions of the "bench" to the reality of the "bedside"?

At first glance, closing the gap may seem to simply be an issue of providing clinicians with adequate information about proven practices and their effectiveness. Given the choice between a treatment that is proven to work and one that is not, won't the overwhelming majority of clinicians choose the proven treatment? However, much information has been provided and yet the gap still exists. Clearly, to understand how to close the gap, we must understand why there is a gap in the first place.

I recently attended the Global Implementation Conference in Washington, DC, to get some answers. What I heard is that no matter how effective a treatment is during testing, if it is not delivered with high quality and sustainability outside of the research lab, it will not be effective. In fact, a new and growing body of literature examines the challenges clinicians face in using research-tested interventions in practice. Several factors have been identified that support adoption and high quality implementation—e.g., pre-implementation training on core components and acceptable adaptations, as well as ongoing implementation assistance and adherence monitoring. However, because the field of implementation science is a relatively new one, there is still no clear evidence on the most effective and cost-efficient way to address these factors in health care delivery.

In our work with intervention developers and clinicians across the country here at the 3-C Institute for Social Development, we have seen an increasing interest in using information technology to improve health. I believe many of the factors shown to foster high quality health care delivery could be accomplished by providing ongoing implementation assistance, such as online supervision and booster training sessions, which could increase the sustainability of a proven practice over time. With the appropriate system protections, health information technology (HIT) could be used to monitor patient adherence to the treatment protocol as well as treatment outcomes.
My Institute colleagues share my enthusiasm for this idea. We have recently received funding from NIH to develop a flexible technology infrastructure called "CenterVention. " This technology infrastructure has been designed to foster high quality implementation and sustainability for research-tested interventions within treatment settings on a broad scale. We are hopeful that this project can serve as a testing ground for using HIT to "close the gap" from research to practice. As we venture forth into this territory, we welcome your comments!

Dr. McMillen is the research director at 3-C Institute for Social Development, overseeing all research activities within the Intervention Division, with a particular emphasis on the development and testing of web-based technology and training resources that support quality intervention implementation in clinical and school settings.

A diverse group of researchers, industry leaders, clinicians, and patient advocates gathered in Washington, D.C. on June 7th to provide input on a national research agenda for patient engagement using health information technology (IT) to support patient-centered communication and care coordination for cancer.

The roundtable was convened by the Office of the National Coordinator for Health IT (ONC), the National Cancer Institute (NCI) at the National Institutes for Health (NIH), and the eHealth Initiative (eHI). More than 40 participants representing a diverse cross-section of the cancer care and IT communities contributed to the discussion of needs and gaps in cancer communication and care coordination, potential health IT solutions, and opportunities for research.

Themes to Help Inform a National Research Agenda

Over the course of the discussion, several themes related to potential needs and opportunities for research emerged.

  • The opportunity to empower an engaged population. Cancer is a condition for which many patients, their families, and caregivers are highly engaged and motivated to act. How can health IT help empower patients and other stakeholders across the cancer continuum?
  • Data interpretation and decision support. Enabling cancer patients to access their data is a crucial step. Beyond this, there is also a need for tools that help patients, families, and caregivers interpret data to support shared decision-making and collaborative self-management.
  • Balancing the needs of individuals and populations. The advancement of genomic sequencing technology and collaborative research in molecular diagnostics offers the promise of accelerating discovery of more targeted and effective therapies. However, it is equally important to understand unique patient needs and opportunities for leveraging health IT based on individual and contextual factors such as age, race-ethnicity, psychosocial factors, and socioeconomic status across the cancer continuum.
  • The influence of culture, incentives, and regulation. Despite the promise of health IT, sociopolitical factors, policy, and financial disincentives have limited the dissemination and impact of existing technologies for communication and coordination in cancer.
  • Examining the future and the present in parallel. National priorities for research must not only consider important questions in the field today, but also how we hope to leverage health IT to improve communication and coordination in cancer care in the next decade.

With these themes as a backdrop, participants offered input to help inform national priorities by proposing a number of potential focus areas for research, including scenarios for assessing the use and impact of health IT; approaches for scaling success and spurring innovation; cultural and policy barriers to adoption; and maximizing the effectiveness of health IT in cancer care for individuals, families, and populations. One of the primary recommendations that arose from the discussion highlighted the need to identify existing tools, applications, and health IT-enabled processes that are successfully promoting cancer communication and care coordination, but would benefit from dissemination and implementation science research to evaluate and scale up effective solutions.

What Comes Next

With the critical issues and innovative ideas shared by participants, ONC, NCI, and eHI emerged from this meeting with a wealth of input that will be used to help inform the development of scientific priority areas for using health IT to improve patient-centered communication and care coordination for cancer. Next steps will include the publication of more detailed proceedings to share the meeting findings, an ONC-led pilot project with the state of Texas, and an environmental scan to help identify high-impact systems, applications, and tools that are successfully using health IT to improve communication and care coordination for cancer. The purpose of the pilot is to give patients access to their information in a format they can download and use it in innovative ways and to assess the impact on their experience of care.

Crowdfunding Healthcare Innovation: Q & A with Alex Fair, CEO of MedStartr

Posted on 11-13-2012
Kasia Galica
National Cancer Institute

In this ICH Blog post, we spotlight MedStartr, a new health-related crowdfunding platform. Crowdfunding for health-related projects is an innovative idea that enables the consumer to decide what health information technology applications or devices are important and ready to come on the market. Approaching the consumer—patients, providers, and families alike—from this angle can potentially give new meaning to the notion of active participation in health.

Q: What is MedStartr?
Alex: MedStartr is a health-specific crowdfunding platform that enables people with ideas or products to be supported by their potential customers. People fund or "back" projects that matter to them. In return the "backers" of the project can get a reward such as the product itself, or something symbolic like a T-shirt, wrist band, or a thank you on a public place. In some cases, non-profits get involved and enable tax-deductible contributions as well. This support—typically first from patients, friends, and family—can lead to a wave of support that may end with partnerships, venture backing, and even sometimes outright acquisitions of the companies. When people really care about an innovative solution or product on MedStartr they can help bring a product to market faster, fund a clinical trial, or start organizations that produce the change that they want to see. It truly puts the power in the hands of the people.
The quintessential example of crowdfunding is a project that originated on Kickstarter. An engineer developed a watch that talked to your iPhone. He called it the Pebble and wanted to do a production run, so he started a project to see if he could sell 5,000 of these before he even had the product. He raised about $10 million and broke records.

MedStartr did not invent or popularize crowdfunding. That honor goes to sites like Indiegogo and Kickstarter. Unfortunately, health-related projects don't do very well on the former and are not allowed on the later. I discovered this in April when I went to put my FairCareMD project onto one of these leading platforms. FairCareMD is an open network for getting care at a fair price, something hundreds of thousands of Americans have used that we never charged for. We had our next version loaded up, but needed to raise funds to add more servers and wanted to experiment with pricing models. Faced with the likely rejection and failure on Kickstarter or Indiegogo, I called my friend and FairCareMD co-founder Mike Pence, who also happened to be the original development lead for Kickstarter. A few days later, we launched an alpha (early version) of MedStartr and sent it to a few dozen friends. They liked it so much they funded the next version, and several asked if they could use it too! We launched the beta(slightly better version) on Independence Day 2012 and had our first 100,000 views and three successful projects a few weeks later. We have not looked back since and have helped a dozen companies find customers, partners, and funding to the tune of hundreds of clients and millions of dollars.

While some projects might work well on Kickstarter, health-related projects are more appropriate for platforms such as MedStartr. Using various social media platforms, our projects bring in a whole community of people that are all about health care innovation—not just entrepreneurs, but leaders of big corporations and government. These stakeholders come in and fund projects. It starts a chain reaction when you get good projects that bring in good press, and bring in more people to fund the care that they care about.
The things that can happen with crowdfunding are incredible. It is fast and can engage not just a couple of people around the country who have large sums of money, but everybody.

Q: What would you say is the primary mission of MedStartr?
Alex: The single mission at MedStartr is to help enable better health outcomes through innovation that is developed faster and better than ever. Our clients embody this mission in myriad ways across every disease, every aspect of care, and every scenario. From rare diseases like fibromuscular dysplasia (FMD) to breast cancer, we have projects designed to advance care, improve support, and save lives.

A great example is the Tampostat project that was developed by a small team at Columbia University. Their product can save over 100,000 women each year from dying due to postpartum hemorrhage after giving birth. Even more amazing, it is simple to use, costs less than $10 to manufacture, and can be used hundreds of times for pennies per use. This is just one of dozens of awe-inspiring ideas that you can find and fund on MedStartr today. We have also helped fund conferences about healthcare, a medical mission to the Dominican Republic, a "GPS" for healthcare, a better bra for women with breast cancer, an iPhone app that helps kids deal with diabetes and stay well, and many more.

The goals of our projects are many, but our mission is simple — help more people live longer, better lives through innovation in healthcare.

Q: Where does patient/consumer engagement come into play here?
Alex: In our opinion consumer and patient involvement is also essential to reducing costs and improving care. Patient engagement is the key to success in crowdfunding. What we find is that when ten or twenty patients stand up and say, "we want this project to exist," it attracts the attention of physicians and partners who will back the projects, select them as partners, or in some cases buy or invest in the startup to enable the next stage. It all starts with patients, though, so even if they are just "liking" the project or giving a few dollars for a T-Shirt, every backing counts. Patients are showing that they can have a profound effect on what products get to market and how fast.

Q: What are some of the early successes of MedStartr?
Alex: We have been very fortunate. Of our first fifteen projects, 7 met their goal on the site and 6 more have had amazing things happen offsite (big partnerships, investments, acquisitions, grants, and more.) Over 70 percent of our projects are getting found and funded faster on MedStartr. This is astounding, even to us.

The first successful project was by David Chase who created Avado, a patient relationship management system that provides health professionals with collaborative care tools to deliver care to their patients while they are outside of the clinical setting. He developed four partnerships, received two grants, and was accepted into the Startup Health Program. MedStartr itself is another example as we crowdfunded ourselves on the site and every day are improving it thanks to the support, suggestions, contributions, and sharing by people all over the world. Another project called FMDChat, by rare disease patient Sarah Kucharski, successfully raised seed funding for a new nonprofit organization dedicated to supporting those affected by FMD. Artist and patient advocate Regina Holliday raised over $15,000 for a new conference called Partnership With Patients. The real success is that we have found a way to help great ideas, projects, and tools get off the ground and into the hands of people that need them.

Q: Beyond these significant accomplishments, what are some of the struggles that MedStartr has had to face?
Alex: As a startup ourselves, we also have limitations. It's challenging to deal with a project that is not getting significant support. A great crowdfunding project needs time and attention and every "overnight success" has many hours of burning the midnight oil behind it. We coach and help our clients as much as we can, but there are limits. Even with good engagement, some people just don't want the solution offered. This can be hard to take. For myself personally, my own FairCareMD project came as a disappointing struggle. I put it up on MedStartr but wasn't able to invest enough effort into promoting it because MedStartr itself was taking all my time. As a result, it didn't get funded through the site. This was disappointing, but through this process we learned the true value of our MedStartr site—creating awareness. So for example, even though FairCareMD did not reach its original funding goals, traffic to the site quintupled and has helped bring more doctors and patients into the platform. We needed to change the business model a little, but not everyone is willing to pivot based upon feedback from the "crowd." As advisors and mentors to many of our clients, we encourage companies to be flexible and be ready to adapt to what their customers really want.

Q: What are some other lessons you've learned thus far?
Alex: One of the most important lessons so far is that crowdfunding isn't just about raising money; it is about raising awareness, adoption, and starting a conversation that leads to many great outcomes. Jen Dyer's project is a great example of this. Dr. Jen Dyer is an endocrinologist and the creator of EndoGoal , a diabetes control mobile phone application. She received feedback from people that loved what she was saying and doing but wanted something a little different. For example, one user said, "This is a great app, and would be even better if it was coupled with remote monitoring that would plug in results from a glucometer/USB attachment." We connected Dr. Dyer with our friends at Agamatrix, developing an automated blood glucose meter. Then we helped navigate the FDA regulation process for the "sidecar" application and a new partnership was formed. What we learned is that we can help companies achieve their goals very rapidly by enabling direct feedback, partnerships, and new thinking that promotes success.

Q: So there is crowdsourcing as well as crowdfunding going on here?
Alex: The idea for MedStartr was first proposed during a healthcare gameification flash mob that we did back in March when my friend Thor Ernsstson (formerly of Zynga) was in town. In addition to the overall concept, we used 99designs.com for our logo and crowdsourced everything from business planning to bug testing. Now, all MedStartr projects get the benefit of crowdsourcing as well as crowdfunding. Monthly showcases by topic and challenges bring in great ideas that we help prepare as projects on the site in conjunction with a "crowd" of mentors expert in every aspect of healthcare innovation. What we call "crowdhelping" begins with a thorough clinical review to ensure scientific validity and includes marketing, videography, business design, PR planning, IP review, and input from some of the best minds in healthcare. Finally, almost all promotion is crowd-based as we rely on every visitor or backer to share projects across their social networks, spreading great ideas virally. Innovators on MedStartr are well supported by our crowds of patients, physicians, scientists, and experts in every field. We've found this to be a powerful approach.

When you can simply imagine a solution and find yourself surrounded by a team of world experts to get the idea off the ground, innovation can happen faster and better than ever. We remove many barriers to innovation and enable the imagination of the crowd to work on the challenging problems of healthcare. Any patient, anywhere, right now, in two hours or less, can create a great MedStartr project and impact healthcare if enough people believe in it. Our showcases and challenges encourage this, attracting and enabling new ideas around important topics such as maternal health, aging and Alzheimer's, cancer, heart disease, and diabetes care.

Q: What are the next steps for MedStartr?
Alex: MedStartr has had a fabulous start, but it is only the beginning. In October, we declared war on maternal mortality and morbidity with the launch of our Saving a Million Mothers projects. Seven projects have been launched, with two fully funded to help women live longer and better lives. November is Aging Gracefully month, and December will be focused on diabetes and devices. We have a story written about us somewhere a few times a week, and projects all across the health tech, biotech, and medical device spectrum are being listed every day. We know we can do more and are planning challenges and events around the world to bring the crowd to crowdfunding. Finally, in December we intend to launch our equity investing platform for successful projects on MedStartr for accredited investors based on recent federal legislation. We want to be ready to help everyone get involved in helping to create and fund the care innovations that they care most about at a whole new level.

Thanks for your time, Alex.

Thanks for your interest in our story. It unfolds every day and we are inspired by the amazing people we get to work with.

Striving for Better & More Accessible Delivery of Information: HHS Digital Strategy

Posted on 11-25-2012
Kasia Galica
National Cancer Institute

Q & A with Richard Stapleton, Deputy Director Web Communications and New Media at the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services

Today's remarkable combination of smart mobile devices, interactive collaboration tools, and cloud computing is greatly influencing the way consumers interface with information. The Federal Government is addressing new expectations by working to deliver and receive digital information and services anytime, anywhere and on any device. The Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) devised a new? Digital Strategy to seize emerging opportunities to engage consumers in the digital age.

Q: What is the HHS Digital Strategy?
Richard: The HHS Digital Strategy is an initiative with a "free the data" approach aimed at increasing the liquidity of information. This effort encompasses all of the traditional topic-specific '.gov' websites. It also includes all forms of data sharing, communication, social media, and third party application programming interfaces (APIs) that allow for mashups —all the while recognizing the growing usage of smart phones and tablets for the flow information. Instead of focusing on processes to improve information technology (IT), the Digital Strategy focuses on 3 outcomes:

  • Enable the American people and an increasingly mobile federal workforce to access information and services anywhere, anytime, on any device.
  • Deliver digital services to the American people smartly, securely, and affordably.
  • Unlock the power of government data to spur innovation.

The overarching goal is to present information for consumers in a way that is fully accessible to them, written in plain language, and brought together in ONE portal.

A Google search on a health topic yields thousands of hits—each one of which can only present a piece of the total picture. Right now the public is playing Where's Waldo? with health information. Our end goal is to create comprehensive topic-based websites that are available in traditional and mobile formats with all the pertinent, reliable, and accurate information found in one place.

Q: What have been some early successes of the HHS Digital Strategy?
Richard: One of the great success stories is flu.gov which deals strictly with influenza, the flu virus. When H1N1 hit and eventually turned into a pandemic, flu.gov was already up and running. We had all the information in one convenient place when all of a sudden the evening news, online news articles, and the newspapers exploded with stories about the flu and its spread. The content was there and the visitation to the site was extraordinary—so it proved its worth at that time as a central source for flu-related information. Another example is foodsafety.gov. This cooperative website brings together food safety information, recalls, and alerts across HHS, USDA, and FDA all in one convenient place. Accessible from a smart phone or tablet, you can get the latest updates on pertinent food safety issues such as the recent salmonella outbreak in peanut butter on demand.

Q: What are some challenges or barriers to implementing this initiative?
Richard: It's a complicated process. For example, we're working on launching a topic specific website on tobacco by the end of the year. We came up with an inventory of 1,100 websites all related to smoking: how-to quit, the health effects of smoking, how-to not start smoking. You name it, it's out there. Not only is there countless information, it also spans a number of major divisions across the federal government. To scan and pull all that information into one platform is a major undertaking requiring the coordinated efforts of many stakeholders on the project.

Q: What are the potential implications of the Digital Strategy for health related research?
With this initiative we take data and research that we have and make it publically available in a format that is not only readable but usable by data and technology innovators for relevant mashups . Using an application programming interface (API), the data can be accessed, applied to population or mapping tables, and presented to create new relevant information or create new opportunities for further research. Making the data available for developers, researchers, and organizations pushes innovation forward and helps to highlight gaps in research.

Q: What are the benefits of the Digital Strategy for the consumer and the government?
Richard: Specifically valuable to us internally is that we can weed out inconsistencies amongst the information scattered across the internet. We find that for whatever reason, some websites have different information. It may be that the content was written at different times and hasn't been updated to match the knowledge that is available now. Finding inconsistent information can be frustrating, misleading, and potentially health-threatening for the consumer. The HHS Digital Strategy seeks to synthesize all the information and deliver the right information to the consumer in a consistent manner.

We also reach out to the public to give us feedback on how to improve the Digital Strategy and ensure we're reaching our intended goals. We have a very interactive blog and pursue an active dialogue on the comments we receive.

Q: What are the next steps for the HHS Digital Strategy?
We've had a great start to this initiative and will continue to engage consumers—utilizing their opinions and feedback to reach our end goals.

I like to use a supermarket model to explain the current state of affairs of information on the web. When you go into a supermarket you can expect that the groceries will be organized by the product, regardless of the manufacturer. If you're looking for cereal, you know that one aisle will have all the cereal—it doesn't matter if it's made by Kellogg or General Mills, or whomever. Right now it's like a trailer truck of grocery cargo dumped all the boxes of food on the highway and we have to sort through it ourselves. People shouldn't need to know who owns the information on tobacco, food safety, the flu, bullying, or cancer. That information should be grouped together in one reliable place and easily accessible on a desktop, laptop, tablet, or smart phone. We're working to make that possible.

ICH Website Retired After Three Active Years

Posted on 01-07-2013

We want to thank the members of the Informatics for Consumer Health (ICH) community for your interest and participation in our mission to bridge the gap between healthcare and information technology by empowering providers and consumers to manage health care.

For the past three years your contributions have had a significant impact on fostering national dialogue and action on the state of informatics for consumer health. Your participation on the ICH main website portal, LinkedIn group, and Twitter handle, has encouraged greater engagement with this important topic. By sharing your relevant news, resources, and related content on the ICH platform you have helped disseminate information crucial for facilitating collaboration to improve consumer health.

As you may know, our primary platform—www.informaticsforconsumerhealth.org—was inspired by the goal of nurturing public-private engagement and collaboration with our Federal Agency partners as initiated by the 2009 Informatics for Consumer Health: Summit on Communication, Collaboration, and Quality. For the past three years this platform has been a central meeting place for stakeholders from various sectors—commercial IT, government, health care, education and research, advocacy—to exchange ideas and resources to bridge information technology and health care with the goal of improving behavioral support for all consumers. Changing health-related behaviors is key to improving health outcomes, with the evidence-base indicating that changes in basic preventive behavior (e.g. smoking cessation, better diet and exercise, and routine screenings) can lead to significant reductions in disability and death due to cancer, heart disease, diabetes, and other diseases.

Our original purpose for maintaining an online rallying place beyond the summit was to help nurture the collaborations that were just beginning to form for the benefit of patients and general consumers. We have been gratified over the past three years to see these collaborations take deep root, and with the formation of the patient engagement initiatives within the HHS Office of the National Coordinator for Health Information Technology, we believe those collaborations will be in good hands. As research continues to grow on informatics for clinical and consumer health, we plan to transition the content from ICH to the National Cancer Institute's (NCI) Health Communication Informatics Research Branch (HCIRB) webpage. The ICH website will no longer be updated on this domain but related efforts will remain a priority for NCI and our agency partners.

Based on the principle of public-private collaboration, consumer health information technology (health IT) holds great promise for encouraging and supporting behavior change. As the science and practice of behavioral and public health informatics evolve, new challenges in translational science such as engagement, design, funding, and evaluation will require attention. To this end, the activities and initiatives frequently presented on the website will continue. New challenges, events, and funding opportunities are sprouting all across the federal landscape. We hope you continue to stay engaged with us on the ICH Twitter handle, LinkedIn group page, and NCI’s HCIRB website.

We would love to hear your feedback. Please share with us how the ICH platform has helped you to collaborate or facilitate your goals for improving consumer health. If you would not like to receive future updates on the evolution of our cross-agency efforts in informatics for consumer health, please click here to opt-out.
