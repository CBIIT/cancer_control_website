TReND: Smoking Cessation across Race/Ethnic and Income Groups of Small Sample Sizes

About the Project

Rationale: Although much progress has been made in the categorization of smokers along a quitting continuum, studies have not evaluated the applicability of accepted predictors of cessation based on national data across small sample size groups, specifically for race/ethnic groups of various income levels. Smokers from various race/ethnic and income groups may not only differ in their distribution along the quitting continuum, but may also differ in their progression to cessation or regression back to smoking. An understanding of the quitting process and related factors across race/ethnic and income groups may provide insight into designing targeted smoking cessation interventions for members of specific race/ethnic and income groups.

Purpose: Through secondary data analyses of the 2003 Tobacco Use Supplements to the Current Population Survey (TUS CPS), TReND’s investigators will evaluate the applicability of previously identified predictors of cessation as precursors to successful cessation across small sample size populations. The recent public availability of the 2003 TUS CPS dataset is timely as it is the first TUS CPS to include extensive questionnaire items on smoking cessation which will allow us to describe patterns of smoking behaviors (e.g., consumption), use of cessation aids (e.g., calling a helpline, using NRT), and factors associated with use or non-use (e.g., physician advice) for small sample size populations, specifically for race/ethnic groups by income strata. The association between these factors and quit attempts/successful cessation across groups will also be examined.

Impact: Results from this study will hopefully have a long-term impact on the smoking cessation field by stimulating additional research and research translation to specific small sample size groups, as well as informing policies and strategies that ultimately impact disparities in tobacco use.
