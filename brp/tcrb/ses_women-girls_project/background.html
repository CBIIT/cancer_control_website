Low Socioeconomic Status (SES) Women & Girls Project

Background

Exposure to tobacco smoke harms almost every organ in the body, causes many diseases, and affects the reproductive system. Lung/bronchial cancer attributed to tobacco use is the number one cause of cancer deaths in women in the U.S and is the most preventable cause of death and disease. Even though the number of smokers in the U.S. is declining, the rates of lung cancer incidence and mortality in women are yet to decline as they have in men. Today, almost one in five women in the U.S. are current tobacco smokers.

In addition to lung and bronchial cancer, tobacco use causes cancer of the mouth, esophagus, pharynx, pancreas, stomach, kidney, bladder, and cervix as well as acute myeloid leukemia. Environmental tobacco smoke (ETS), or second hand smoke, is a known cause of lung cancer and coronary heart disease in women, including those who have never smoked. ETS exposure during pregnancy can lead to reduced infant birth weight and intrauterine growth retardation.

This year alone, almost half of the estimated deaths due to lung and bronchial cancer will be women. Smokers who quit significantly reduce their health risks, including premature loss of life.

Sources: Annual Report to the Nation on the Status of Cancer, 1975-2002, J Natl Cancer Inst 2005 Oct 5;97(19):1407-27; The Health Consequences of Smoking: A Report of the Surgeon General, May 2004; Women and Smoking: A Report of the Surgeon General, March 2001.

Women, Smoking and Age Group

Approximately 20% of women aged 18-64 in the U.S. are currently smokers. This number drops to less than 10% at the age of 65. In all age ranges, the percentage of men who currently smoke is higher in men than women.


Source: Cigarette Smoking Among Adults - United States, 2004, Morbidity and Mortality Weekly Report 2005, 54:1121-1124, Centers for Disease Control and Prevention.

Women, Smoking and Race

Among female smokers, American Indians/Alaska Natives have the highest smoking prevalence, while Asians have the lowest.


Source: Cigarette Smoking Among Adults - United States, 2004, Morbidity and Mortality Weekly Report 2005, 54:1121-1124, Centers for Disease Control and Prevention

Education, Poverty Status, & Tobacco Use

A woman's level of education and poverty status significantly impact her smoking behavior. Women who have more years of education and incomes at or above the poverty level smoke less than women who have fewer years of education and incomes below the poverty level.
