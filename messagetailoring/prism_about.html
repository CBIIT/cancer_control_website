What was special about the project?
The project, “Promoting Informed Decision Making about Mammography” was conducted as part of a Cancer Prevention Research Unit funded by the National Cancer Institute. The program was composed of four projects and four cores, which provided shared resources for all the projects. The overarching goal was to advance the field of cancer risk communications by identifying how people perceive cancer-related risks and how those perceptions can be altered to promote informed decisionmaking and behavior change. In PRISM, we tested new ways of communicating concepts related to decisionmaking and breast cancer screening, such as the risk of getting breast cancer, the risk of dying of breast cancer, and the benefits and harms of mammography for women of different ages.

Why was this project done?
At project onset, recent controversies about breast cancer screening led to confusion among women and health care providers alike about the age and frequency that women should get mammograms. Some professional organizations recommended that only women aged 50 and older be screened, while others advised that women in their 40s also be screened. The communications model for mammography must change from persuasion or prescription to informed decisionmaking by patients, in collaboration with health care providers. However, informed decisionmaking is easier said than done. This project developed and tested concrete strategies to promote informed decisionmaking about mammography. The lessons learned in this project can be transferred to other areas in which there is uncertainty and patients are advised to make informed decisions.

What is an informed decision?
An informed decision about mammography is made when a woman clearly understands the procedure and recognizes the risks and benefits associated with mammography. Further she is aware of the alternatives and is autonomous in making her decision.

What was the overall aim of the project?
Women receive different and contradictory messages concerning breast cancer risk and appropriate screening practices. Many over- or underestimate their risk for breast cancer as well as the benefits of mammography. Consequently, there is a need to communicate accurate, understandable, and compelling risk messages that alleviate women’s anxieties about breast cancer risk and motivate them to take appropriate actions.

Specifically this project intended to help women make informed decisions about mammography by:

  • Communicating the benefits and harms of mammography for women in their 40s and 50s
  • Providing a woman’s personal risk of getting breast cancer based on the Gail Model and realigning misperceptions about breast cancer risk
  • Giving advice to help women overcome their barriers to screening

We expected to:

  • Improve the accuracy of women’s personal perceptions about breast cancer risk
  • Increase women’s comprehension of mammography-related information
  • Improve women’s satisfaction with mammography decisions
  • Increase the number of women who obtain regular mammograms

Who participated in the project?
Approximately 1,300 women members of the Personal Care Plan of Blue Cross and Blue Shield of North Carolina (BCBSNC) participated. Women were recruited from a sample that was categorized into two age groups reflecting different decisionmaking issues: 40-44 and 50-54. The sample was stratified on age and mammography adherence status using BCBS claims data. We oversampled women with no record of a mammogram in the past year for women aged 50-54 or 2 years for women aged 40-44. Women were randomized in to three groups: Usual Care (UC - standard BCBSNC mammography reminder card mailed on woman's birthday), Tailored Feedback (Print) (TP), and Tailored Feedback (Print) with Telephone Counseling (TP+TC). They received a baseline telephone survey and 12-month and 24-month follow-up telephone survey (See PRISM Flowchart.)

How was the project evaluated?
Baseline data was collected prior to the first intervention. (See Baseline Survey) The intervention groups received a tailored booklet with or without telephone counseling. After 12 months, the groups were reassessed with a follow-up interview. (See 12-Month Survey) Then, the intervention groups received a tailored newsletter with or without telephone counseling based on the updated data. After 12 months, the groups were again reassessed with a final follow-up interview. (See 24-Month Survey) The effectiveness of tailored materials and telephone calls were compared with usual care. It was hypothesized that women who received tailored materials and tailored telephone calls would be more likely to make informed decisions and get regular mammograms than would those who did not receive the tailored materials and/or calls.

Among the variables we assessed were:

  • Comprehension of the benefits and harms of mammography
  • Accuracy of women’s perceived personal risk for developing breast cancer
  • Intention to get a mammogram
  • Adherence to mammography
  • Satisfaction with decisionmaking
