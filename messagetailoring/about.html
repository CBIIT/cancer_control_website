Health Message Tailoring: About



Tailored health communications is a promising and innovative approach that can be used to address many public health issues.

A subset of health communications, tailored communications allow practitioners to generate highly customized messages on a large scale by using computers.

  • Tailored communications are created especially for an individual based on knowledge of that person.
  • Information about a person may be gathered through a survey or personal interview.
  • The person's answers to the survey or interview are entered into a computer, which draws from a "library" of possible messages to create materials that directly address the person's needs, interests, or concerns.

Empirical research shows that tailored print materials are more effective than non-tailored ones in helping people change health behaviors such as smoking and physical activity.

Over the last 10 to 15 years, "tailored health communications" has emerged as a promising, innovative approach to addressing public health issues. Dr. Matthew Kreuter of the Health Communications Research Laboratory at Saint Louis University, and David Farrell of People Designs, a design and materials development firm, define tailored health communications as "any combination of information and behavior change strategies intended to reach one specific person, based on characteristics that are unique to that person, related to the outcome of interest, and derived from an individual assessment."

A subset of the larger field of health communications, tailored communications allow practitioners to generate highly customized messages on a large scale by using computers. This approach is distinct from more familiar, but less personalized, methods of developing "targeted" materials (in which messages are prepared using information about population segments) and "personalized" materials (which use a person's name to draw attention to a generic message). Because they can address cognitive and behavioral patterns as well as individual demographic characteristics, tailored materials are more sophisticated than targeted ones.

Tailored communications are created especially for an individual based on knowledge of that person. This information may be gathered through a survey or through a brief personal interview with a professional. The results of these questions are entered into a computer, which draws from a "library" of possible messages to create materials that directly address the individual's needs, interests, or concerns. Once a program has been developed for a certain health issue, it can be used to produce tailored print materials with the potential to reach large populations. Thus, tailored communications are an ideal tool to address a variety of public health issues.

Although the field is still in its infancy, empirical research shows that tailored print materials are more effective than non-tailored ones in helping people change health behaviors such as smoking, diet, and physical activity. According to Skinner et al., who published the first scientific review of tailoring research, "tailored print communications have demonstrated an enhanced ability to attract notice and readership…are more effective than non-tailored communications for influencing health behavior change…(and) can be an important adjunct to other intervention components."

References

Kreuter, M., Farrell, D., Olevitch, L., & Brennan, L. (2000). Tailored Health Messages: Customizing Communication with Computer Technology. Mahwah, New Jersey: Lawrence Erlbaum Associates, Publishers.

Skinner, C.S., Campbell, M.K., Rimer, B.K., Curry, S., & Prochaska, J.O. (1999). "How effective is tailored print communication?" Annals of Behavioral Medicine, 21,(4), 290-8.
