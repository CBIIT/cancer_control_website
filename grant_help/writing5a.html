The Grant Writing Process - Writing

Arrangement and Organization of Information

Usually, ideas and facts gained from original research and invention have to be arranged for clarity and effect before being written into a grant application. One way to begin this task of arrangement is to order researched or invented material to meet the reader's needs and expectations. Then, once an ordering principle is established, the researcher can develop an outline, which will serve as the basis for drafts of the document.

Thus, facing the information that he or she has accumulated, the writer still has to impose order, arranging it according to some principle. Generally speaking, two factors that influence the order in which the information will be presented are "time-and-space" and the "effect" the writer wants to achieve.

Time and Space Ordering

A reader/reviewer should be able to proceed through the text without encountering mind-baffling gaps or jumps in logic stemming from the writer's disregarding of important environmental factors such as time and space.

Ordering information and sections chronologically or spatially can result in a grant proposal that is arranged naturally according to the way experience is arranged. By presenting these events chronologically (according to their order in time), the writer is enabling the reader to understand them more fully.

A writer may also need to describe an object or area spatially so that the reader can visualize it clearly.

Organizing for Effect

When researchers need to order document sections and information for effect, they generally take advantage of the logical connections between ideas and facts and how the reader perceives these connections. Information often flows logically from more general ideas to specifics that develop and support those ideas. Or it can flow in the opposite direction from the specifics to the generalizations they support.

Ordering Techniques:General to Specific and Specific to General

Both general-to-specific and specific-to-general ordering techniques, then, have their place and function in the logical arrangement of grant applications. A variation of this type of logical ordering may be achieved by simply determining what information is most important to the writer's point and most convincing to the audience and arranging the presentation so that the reader experiences this material either first or last in the document. Often readers are busy and hurried in their reading habits, so what is most important and most convincing has to appear early in the grant application narrative.

Otherwise, it might be missed. If, on the other hand, the reader has more time to consider the writer's points, then the writer might organize the document so that the reader proceeds through the less convincing or less important information to the more salient and persuasive ideas that appear late in the document. This way, the reader emerges from reading with the strongest arguments in mind.

Techniques for Ordering for Importance:

#1 Most Important Point or Fact —> Point(s) of Secondary Importance —> Least Important Point or Fact

#2 Least Important Point or Fact —>Point(s) of Secondary Importance —> Most Important Point or Fact
