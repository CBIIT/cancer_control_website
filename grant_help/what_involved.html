NIH Funding Instruments and Grant Mechanisms*

Grants are only one of three major funding instruments that NIH uses to support extramural research. Other funding instruments include contracts and cooperative agreements. The distinctions between these are shown in Table 1.

Table 1. Funding Instruments

Instrument Approach to funding
Grant Investigator determines the research topic, design, and approach.
Contract Government identifies a research need, and sets requirements.
Cooperative Agreement Both the awarding Institute/Center (IC) and the recipient carry out the project's activities.

NIH also uses 4 different avenues for funding extramural research. For some avenues of funding, the investigator determines the topic and research approach; NIH determines other avenues for extramural research. These are shown in Table 2.

Table 2. Avenues for Extramural Research Funding

Avenue Initiator  
Investigator-Initiated Research Unsolicited The investigator initiates the research and submits a grant application within an area that is relevant to the NIH.
Program Announcement (PA) Solicited NIH solicits grant applications or cooperative agreements in a given research area (i.e., a new, ongoing or expanded interest and/or high priority program).
Request for Applications (RFA) Solicited NIH solicits research grant applications for a one-time competition on a specific topic.
Request for Proposals (RFP) Solicited NIH solicits research proposals for a one- time competition on a specific IC topic.

*Adapted from "Grant Writing Tips Sheets: Choosing an Appropriate NIH Funding Instrument and Funding Mechanism." http://grants1.nih.gov/grants/choosing_fundInstrument.doc

  • Traditional Research Project Grant (R01): awarded to eligible institutions on behalf of a principal investigator to support a discrete project related to the investigator's area of interest and competence. These grants make up the largest category of NIH funding. http://grants.nih.gov/grants/funding/r01.htm


  • Small Research Grant (R03): supports small research projects that can be carried out in a short period of time with limited resources. Examples include pilot or feasibility studies; secondary analysis of existing data; development of research methodology and/or technology. http://grants.nih.gov/grants/funding/r03.htm


  • Exploratory/Developmental Research Grant (R21): seeks to broaden the base of inquiry in fundamental biomedical research by encouraging applications for research projects that involve a high degree of innovation and novelty. NIH provides pilot support for potentially ground-breaking ideas and methods that: lack sufficient preliminary data to establish feasibility; could have a major impact on biomedical research if successfully demonstrated; and fall within the areas supported by the awarding IC. http://grants.nih.gov/grants/funding/r21.htm


  • Scientific Meeting Support (R13): NIH provides support for scientific meetings, conferences, and workshops that are relevant to its scientific mission. Any U.S. institution or organization, including an established scientific or professional society, is eligible to apply. http://grants.nih.gov/grants/funding/r13/index.htm


  • Small Business Innovation Research Grant (SBIR: R43/R44) and Small Business Technology Transfer Grant (STTR: R41/R42): made to eligible, domestic, for-profit small businesses that are conducting innovative research with potential for commercialization. http://grants.nih.gov/grants/funding/sbir.htm

†Special action is required if the grant is to exceed $500K for any given year.

NIH Grant Submission Procedures§

Note: If you are submitting a request for $500,000 or more for a year, contact the Institute/Center staff at least 6 weeks before submitting your request and get their agreement to accept the assignment. (10/16/2001 NIH Guide Notice: http://grants.nih.gov/grants/guide/notice-files/NOT-OD-02-012.html)

When you are ready to send your application to the Center for Scientific Review, remember to send it via courier or the USPS-do not deliver it in person! For more details about delivering your application, see the NIH Guide Notice dated April 22, 2003 (http://grants.nih.gov/grants/guide/notice-files/NOT-OD-03-040.html). The current mailing address is:

Center for Scientific Review
National Institutes of Health
Suite 1040
6701 Rockledge Drive MSC 7710
Bethesda, MD 20892-7710

An application is on time if it is either:

  • Received by the published receipt date, or
  • Mailed on or before the published receipt date, and proof of timely mailing is provided

Proof of timely mailing consists of:

  • A dated U.S. Postal Service postmark, or
  • A dated receipt from a commercial carrier or the U.S. Postal Service
  • Private metered postmarks are not acceptable.

When a receipt date falls on a weekend or holiday, the receipt date is the following business day.

§Source: NIH Office for Extramural Research, http://grants.nih.gov/grants/funding/submissionschedule.htm

Late Applications
CSR does not grant permission ahead of time to submit a late application. Late applications are accepted only in extenuating circumstances. If an application is submitted late, you must include with the signed, completed application a cover letter explaining the reasons for the delay. Late applications are evaluated on an individual basis taking into account the reasons provided. Contacting the Division of Receipt and Referral in advance will not influence the acceptance of a late application. See the 01/27/2005 NIH Guide Notice, NIH Policy on Late Submission of Grant Applications (http://grants.nih.gov/grants/guide/notice-files/NOT-OD-05-030.html).

Talk with appropriate partners and collaborators

It is important to plan and prepare carefully before you start writing your application. Be aware that grant applications can take up to six months to write, and another nine months for the funding to go through if your application is accepted.

Consider the following as you create the timeline for writing your grant application:

  • Since you will need an official signature from your institution's business office, make sure you know how much time they will need to provide it, and whether they have any deadlines.


  • If you are doing research with animals or human subjects, you will need to prepare special documentation, for example IRB certification, informed consent forms, and/or Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee (IACUC) certification. You may also need to submit a data sharing plan, or a plan for sharing model organisms.


  • You should have your application reviewed internally, and have the time to make necessary edits.


  • A good way to get feedback on your planning process is to invite senior colleagues to be on your own small review committee. This is a great opportunity to share your ideas and listen to theirs before you start writing your application. Take some time to write up the specific aims of your project, as agreed upon with the committee. Discussing these with committee members, and having them review your application after you write it, will help ensure that you are writing and organizing well.
