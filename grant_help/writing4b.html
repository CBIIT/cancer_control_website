The Grant Writing Process - Design

Hypothesis Development

Topics

  1. The importance of recent research or landmark studies
    • Using a "logical tree" will aid hypothesis development and may be used to design a study that will help rule out competing hypotheses.
    • The results of the study should have heuristic value and contribute to the scientific body of knowledge
    • Accurately identifying shortcomings in other research will help provide the "inspiration" for identifying the issues that need addressing in the field
    • Few studies should be developed only to replicate past research
      • waste of money
      • waste of time
    • The general design of a previously conducted study can be replicated to assess:
      • Different dosages
      • Different intervention techniques
      • Different subsets of individuals / animals / cells / drugs. In human research, subsets different from a previous study can be selected on the basis of:
        • Age
        • Ethnicity
        • SES
        • Urban versus rural residence

  2. Basic issues surrounding the hypothesis
    • Note the difference between the alternative hypothesis (HA) versus the null hypothesis (HO)
    • HA is usually found at the end of the study's introduction
    • The hypothesis tested may not be the HA that the authors suggest
    • Type I error is defined as accepting HA when HA is not true
    • Type II error is defined as rejecting HO when HA is true

  3. The importance of getting input from others about the proposed study
    • Reviewers of the study do not necessarily have to be your friends
    • A critical evaluation by the reviewer should be assured
    • A written assessment of both strengths and weaknesses of the study should be obtained

  4. Reasons for negative outcomes (lack of significant results)
    • One reason that no significant relationship is found in a study is that, in reality, no significant relationship exists. However, negative results often result from weaknesses in the study design:
      • The investigator used poor quality measurement instruments
      • The sample may have been biased
      • The sample size may not have been sufficiently large
      • The study was biased against finding a significant result
      • Third variable effects may have influenced the results
      • The relationship of interest may exist in some populations but not others
      • Error may have influenced the results. Error can occur:
        • During collection
        • During data entry
        • During measurement
        • In the response
        • In poor quality equipment or measures
        • From poor calibration of equipment
        • From environmental factors
        • From social desirable response
        • From random error
        • In computer programming
      • To improve the quality of a grant application, prospective researchers should consider addressing the above weaknesses in study design to convince the review committee that the these problems will be controlled as much as possible

Discussion

Rarely can a study be developed without considering the results obtained in past research. Because no study is perfect or addresses all the questions that may arise about a given research topic, fresh empirical questions can be inspired from articles published in peer reviewed journals or from presentations at professional meetings.

To develop a testable, timely hypothesis, investigators are advised to use a logical progression of ideas. Whereas a study or a brief series of studies can be used as a "seed" for a specific research idea, investigators should become highly familiar with bulk of the research in the area of interest. This knowledge will prevent redundancies across studies and provide the researcher with the problems encountered and knowledge yielded by other researchers.

Because researchers generate and refine ideas based previous work, hypotheses tend to diverge or "branch out" from the research question that spawned the subsequent research. Eventually, many of these ideas become more refined, yielding more branches of the research tree. As the field develops, some branches will develop no further, perhaps because the concepts associated with that branch are no longer of interest to funding agencies or less frequently to qualified researchers. That is, the information that is currently available is all that is required for the time being, or for other less obvious reasons, the domain is no longer one that attracts research activity. Therefore, the key element in generating a hypothesis is to develop an empirical question that will advance a field of knowledge in a timely manner; a study designed only to replicate a previous study is seldom of value and is usually considered a waste of time and money by funding institutions and other researchers.

Additional steps — Merely a very preliminary step has been accomplished when the scientist has decided on the research question to be studied. That is, researchers should develop competing hypotheses which can be integrated into the study design and which can be tested to maximize the strength of the study's findings. For example, most social scientists are at least somewhat knowledgeable about using multivariate analyses to control for the influence of variables that may confound the results. Careful consideration about which variables to include in the multivariate equation, however, is sometimes lacking yielding results that are of dubious value. However, including data in the multivariate equation variable identified as having the potential to confound the results will prevent criticisms that the results were biased at that level. Therefore, a researcher should anticipate alternative explanations for the expected findings, and design the study in such a way that these other hypotheses can be tested and presumably ruled out. Thus, a research study might be considered as a series (if it has several components) or one in a series of experiments (or studies), each one building on knowledge from the previous one.

Of course, every investigator wants to provide information that is accurate—(i.e., avoid committing a Type I or a Type II error). That is, the conclusions about relationships should not be considered "real" if the relationships are "false," and not be considered "false" if the relationships truly exist. Neither a Type I nor a Type II error is desirable, but committing a Type I error is somewhat less agreeable than producing a Type II error.

These problematic factors include:

  • Poor quality instruments
  • Sample not representative of the population of interest
  • Sample size insufficient
  • Study design biased against finding a significant result
  • Third variable effects influencing the results
  • A relationship of interest in some populations but not others

Error can occur during:

  • Data collection
  • Data entry
  • Measurement
  • The response
  • From environmental factors
  • From social desirability
  • From random error
  • From poor quality equipment or measures
  • From poor calibration of equipment
  • In computer programming

Certainly these factors, some more than others, may also influence the data toward a significant result; however, in general these considerations will decrease the likelihood of obtaining significant result for reasons discussed elsewhere. Of course, despite the above discussion, the possibility remains when a negative result is obtained that the findings represent reality and that no significant relationship exists.

If an investigator is writing a grant application, addressing the potential problems listed above may enhance the chances for obtaining grant funding. For example, a good strategy during the writing of the grant is to demonstrate both the knowledge that the above problems may exist in a given study as well as suggest solutions to respond to these difficulties. The researcher should justify using particular instruments by providing information on the measure's reliability and validity; a suitable discussion about the appropriateness of the source for the sample is also needed, and power calculations should be included to demonstrate the size of the sample will be adequate, etc.
