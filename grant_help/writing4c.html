Discussion

The Characteristics of the Sample

Before the sample can be selected, the target population must be identified. The target population should be specifically defined. Naturally, the study population should resemble the target population as closely as possible because the results from the study should be generalizable. If the subjects in the sample have characteristics different from the target population, these differences must be considered potential confounders. Confounding variables influence the results, but are not directly related to the hypothesis.

Controlling for, or partialling out the effects of these confounders related to a biased sample is virtually impossible. Therefore, if the study sample does not represent the population of interest, the study will likely yield information of application only to the study's subjects, and the findings will be of little value elsewhere.

Once the target population is identified, the researcher should develop specific guidelines about subjects' eligibility criteria. These guidelines would reflect both inclusionary criteria and exclusionary criteriaâ€”characteristics that will make the person ineligible for the study. For an example, in study of the effects of a single moderate dose an illicit drug on cognitive functioning, the inclusionary criteria for subjects might be: 1) between the ages of 12 and 18 (an age arbitrarily chosen, and for this example, in which the risk of using this drug is highest); 2) of at least a normal level of cognitive ability; and 3) male (because males are much more likely to use the imaginary drug in question). The exclusionary criteria could be: 1) not having been unconscious for more than 7 minutes after a trauma to the head (to eliminate the possibility of otherwise latent organic damages confounding the results); and 2) no history of drug abuse (to rule out the possibility that chronic drug use exacerbates or magnifies the effects from an acute dosage of the drug).

Thus, the study sample should be selected based on rigorous specific criteria, otherwise the results may not be replicable by other researchers. An indication about the representativeness of the sample could come from a comparison of the characteristics of those who refused to be included in the study to those who participated as subjects. In longitudinal studies, similar analyses can be conducted to determine if the people who drop out of the study were different than those who remained in the study. If the study participants were found to be different from those who were not in the study, the results could be biased because the subjects who were potentially the "worst cases" were not included in the study.

When the outcome variable is related to a sensitive topic such as drug abuse, potential subjects may refuse participation or already recruited subjects may decline further participation because they feel guilty about engaging in an illicit activity. Not obtaining data from these individuals will likely bias the outcome of the study.

The Sample Size

Closely related to the generalizability of the study group to the target population is the size of the sample. Assuming no bias was present during the selection of the sample, the larger the sample size, the more likely it will have characteristics similar to that of the larger target group. Small samples, because of chance selection, may yield observations that are not representative of the population of interest. For this reason, the results of most studies with small samples should be regarded with some caution. Although statistical tests with small samples require a relationship in which the magnitude is relatively large before a significant result is found, the potential remains for the results from small studies to be ungeneralizable to the target population.

Power calculations should be generated at some point when the study is being designed to determine the number of subjects that should be recruited into the study. The results of power calculations help ensure that significant results are found if a relationship truly exists. Any one of several computer packages can be used to yield estimates of the power of the test. Statistical power is based on:

  • Expected magnitude of the relationship
  • Number of subjects
  • Expected variability of the data
  • Level of alpha
  • Type of hypothesis (one versus two-tailed)

Any one of these values can be calculated if the information from the others is available. Typically, investigators know:

  1. The expected magnitude of the relationship found in previous studies, although some tendency to be overly optimistic about the strength of the relationship that would be found in the proposed study is not unusual among investigators. Such optimism is nonetheless ill-advised


  2. The level of alpha, which is the p value at which a finding will be accepted as statistically significant (e.g., the probability that the observed relationship is not likely to be a result of chance or of random fluctuations in the data), usually set at .05


  3. The variability of the data (e.g., for continuous scales indicated by the standard deviations)


  4. The direction of the hypothesis, whether it is one-tailed (e.g., the treatment group will have a better outcome than the control group) or two-tailed (e.g., which group will have a better outcome is unknownâ€”the new treatment will differ in efficacy from the group receiving the current standard of treatment). Typically, the investigator will need to calculate either the number of subjects needed based on an acceptable risk for a Type II error (usually defined as .20 which means that the researcher has an 80% chance of identifying a truly significant result) or if the researcher knows the number of subjects available, the risk for a Type II error can be estimated. Unfortunately, researchers are often surprised to find that the initially proposed sample size is not sufficient to yield an acceptably low risk of a Type II error.


The study should not be conducted if the risk for a Type II error is too high (i.e., more than 20%). In such a case the researcher, support staff, and the subjects would be wasting their time contributing to a study that will likely be of little value. Worse, if the study were conducted and if the results of the study were published, the findings may be misleading and could be widely distributed among the research community, diverting future research from more worthy projects.

Methods of Selecting Potential Subjects

The method of selecting subjects is a crucial part of the research project. First the investigator must determine whether the subjects of interest are accessible, and whether the source of subjects would provide a biased sample. Each method of recruitment that is outlined below has advantages and disadvantages; however, regardless of the methods used, the researcher should be as certain as possible that the sample is not biased.

Random Samples

The most convincing method of obtaining an unbiased study population is to use some type of random selection from the target population. If the sample is large enough, the group of subjects will not likely differ much from the target population, thereby virtually ensuring generalizability of the data. Random selection is when sampling unit has an equal chance of being selected; usually an individual subject is a "sampling unit", but can also be a family, organization, or some other group of people. If the study is focused on treatment efficacy, randomization can be used to assign the available subjects to the various study groups. The term "random selection" should not suggest that the subjects were identified in a willy-nilly fashion. In fact, a considerable amount of effort might be expended to assure potential critics of the study that the random selection scheme has worked. Random number generators may suffer a glitch or a propensity to develop patterns of numbers that may conceivably bias the results. Rolling a die may produce a biased sample if the die has pitted rather than painted dots; when the die is pitted, "6" is most likely to be rolled because of the additional weight on the opposite side of the die, the "1"; therefore obtaining a higher percentage of even numbers is more likely as is higher percentage of high numbers (4, 5 and 6). Because group assignment may be based on the result of the odd or even roll, one group may be overrepresented, presenting problems in the analysis of the data. Random telephone dialers provide biased data for some studies because not all people, particularly American Indians and some recent immigrants, have telephones.

Frequently, investigators prefer to use a simplified method of sampling, such as systematic sampling. Instead of a random number table to select subjects, a list of potential subjects is obtained, and the names are counted. The number of potential subjects is divided by the number of subjects needed for the data analysis yielding the variable n. The investigator then selects every nth person on the list. For example, if a list has 2100 names, and a power analysis indicates that the investigators needs 100 subjects to participate in the study, every twenty-first subject is approach to participate in the study. Usually instead of counting from the first subject, a random number between 1 and 100 (or some other arbitrary number that is reasonably high) will be generated, and the counting begins from the random number. If, for whatever reason, the number of subjects needed for the study is not obtained (e.g., the subject refused to participate) with the first pass through the data, the process can begin again, using a different "seed" numberâ€”the number at which the first subject is picked.

This procedure will not work well, however, if the subjects are arranged in some type of biased manner (e.g., in a prison population, every twenty-first prisoner on the list is an axe murderer; every twenty-second prisoner an arsonist, etc.).

Another method of obtaining subjects is by using cluster sampling. For cluster sampling, an organizational unit is systematically divided into its logical smaller components; randomization is accomplished at each level until the smallest organizational unit is selected and the sample requirements are satisfied. For an example, a study is conducted by a large corporation. First departments (representing the largest sampling unit) are randomly selected; next divisions in those departments are randomly identified and then sections in the divisions are chosen. All individuals in the selected sections would then be assessed. The potential for bias exists, then, if the various departments (or the divisions or sections) contain individuals with characteristics different from the other departments.

However, random samples will sometimes yield a study population that resembles the target group, but some of the subgroups of interest will have sample sizes that are too small for data analysis. If the investigator is interested in these subgroups, a stratified random sample should be obtained.

In this case, each individual within the subgroups has an equal probability of being chosen, but the smaller groups of people are disproportionately oversampled. Recently conducted national surveys are stratified random samples; if a random sample were obtained, the number of subjects in some ethnic subgroups would be too small to provide enough information for analysis. Consequently, instead of a sample that contains 13% Blacks, this subgroup could be oversampled to yield a study group that may contain 26% Blacks. Whites may have contributed 70% to a random sample but in this example may only contribute 57% to a stratified random sample; however, this percentage of Whites should produce sufficient numbers of subjects on which the data can be analyzed for that subgroup.

Regardless of the method of randomization, one or two trials of the method should be conducted before subjects are obtained or assigned to treatment groups. Despite the success of these trials, a biased sample can still be obtained from the luck, (or the lack of it), of the draw. Usually, though, obtaining a large number of subjects will yield a sample that resembles the target population for most variables. Smaller samples are more susceptible to suffering from bias, regardless of the sampling technique.

An example of a method that will not result in a random assignment of subjects into groups is a physician who wants to assess the efficacy of an intervention technique. Rather than developing a random assignment process, the physician assigns the first patient of the day to the experimental condition, the second patient to the control condition, and the subsequent patients are alternated between the two groups throughout the day. The next day, the process is repeated. Unfortunately, the physician may have developed a tendency to see patients with the most challenging condition first. Therefore, the experimental condition continually receives the most difficult patient of the day, biasing the sample.

Nonrandom Samples

Often, a random sample is not practical or, for other reasons, cannot be obtained. A constraint to obtaining a random sample is having a list of sampling units from which the selection will be made. Alternative sampling techniques are available and can yield high quality data. However, again, the investigator must take the appropriate steps to ensure that the data are not biased.

Possibly the method of sampling that is most likely to yield a biased sample is in recruiting individuals as they come to a clinic, a university classroom, or some other institution. Obtaining a sample in such a way is usually done by convenience because the investigator usually has ready access to those populations; thus the sample is called a convenience sample. Individuals who are recruited in this manner may have characteristics that are not consistent with those of the target population. Subjects in a convenience sample may be, and probably are, different from the population of interest in terms of socioeconomic status, rate of adherence to treatment regimens, level of cognitive functioning, etc.

For some research questions such as drug abuse patterns in a community, identifying or contacting potential subjects for a randomization scheme is impossible. For example, in a study of drug use among street gangs, the most practical means by which potential sources of information can be identified may be to contact a known source who will direct the investigator to other subjects. These subjects will hopefully be cooperative in providing data and additional sources of contact. This method of sampling is called "snowball" sampling, and is typical in ethnographic research. Of course, the quality of the sample may depend on the initial contact, but presumably after several subsequent contacts are made, the researcher has identified individuals who will provide the highest quality information that is least susceptible to bias.

Another strategy to obtain subjects is by quota sampling. In this strategy, power calculations are generated and a desired number of people who fall into a predefined category is obtained. For example, a telephone interviewer using a random dialing computer program may then contact a large number of individuals. Based on a short questionnaire, potential subjects can be screened. Categories are based on well defined characteristics (e.g., gender, age, income, and/or ethnic group). Once a quota is filled, no other information is needed or obtained from individuals with that set of characteristics.

Other Considerations

Many investigators are a bit myopic when criticizing their own studies and, in particular, do not readily recognize the instances when their sample is atypical or biased. Nonetheless, investigators should be aware if their sample is composed of individuals with unusual characteristics (e.g., they are all being treated for psychosis) that are not germane to the study and may influence the results. Sometimes unusual characteristics that influence data are not immediately apparent; however, the investigator should be in a position to determine if there is any factor that may be affect the subjects' responses. For example, assessing psychological affect in a population living close to Three Mile Island within a few days of the now famous nuclear accident would surely yield interesting data; however, the researchers should not imply that the data would be representative of a typical community that had not been exposed to the incident.

Further, investigators should be cognizant of the fact that the design itself may influence responses. For example, videotaping an interaction between admitted drug users may yield data of questionable validity because such subjects may be reticent to discuss candidly (or document) their past history of drug use.
