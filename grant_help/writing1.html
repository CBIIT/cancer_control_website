How Good Are Your Research Skills?

1. Have you completed a Ph.D. or equivalent degree with a research emphasis?

Most R01 grant recipients hold a Ph.D. or equivalent degree. If you do not hold this expected degree, then greater justification will be required to show how you might qualify as a scientist and research administrator.

2. Do you belong to any scientific societies?

Membership in scientific societies suggests a current understanding of research and scholarship. Peer review committee members may also infer from your active membership in these groups that you are well-informed on topics of current interest that relate both to your career and your grant application.

3. Can you identify 10 principal researchers in your research field?

If you cannot name 10 relevant figures in your research field, this suggests that you may lack the requisite knowledge to construct significant research questions and appropriate designs within the context of the field.

4. Have you presented original research to peers at a national scientific conference?

Participation in national conferences suggests that you are familiar with the work of other scholars who share your research interests; have presented your own work to your peers and/or served as a discussant on relevant panels, and are an active member of a particular scholarly community.

5. Have you received scholarships, grants, or awards for research training or research development?

Prior funding in this area, especially if it was awarded competitively, is likely to increase your chances of NIH funding because it reveals that you have the level of education and experience to conduct scholarly research. Non-peer reviewed funding, such as scholarships and honors, also serve to demonstrate your commitment to and expertise in the research arena.

6. Have you ever supervised research assistants?

Your role as an educator and administrator may prove important in certain types of granting situations and suggests a greater overall experience and competence needed to complete the proposed research successfully.

7. Can you identify four or more refereed journals in your research field?

Knowledge of articles appearing in refereed journals will help to demonstrate the relevant background and significance of your proposed research. The peer review committee may also assume that you will publish the findings of your research and will thus be looking for evidence of your informed perspective.

8. Have you published in a refereed journal in the past 5 years?

Most grantees are expected to have published recently in peer refereed journals. This is one indication that you are not only likely to be competitive, but also suggests that you will continue to share your work publicly.

9. Were you the senior author of a scientific article?

Rank in authorship suggests a relative importance of your research ability and your demonstrates your willingness to work with others. Peer review groups are looking for demonstrated success and ability.

10. Have you received peer review grant support in the past?

Prior research awards from organizations that employ a peer review mechanism to determine scientific acceptability (for example, PHS fellowships, small grants and career awards; foundation support; and/or State or institution grants) have a positive impact on NIH Initial Review Group (IRG) members. It is important that you list any participation you may have either as the direct recipient of such an award or as a member of a research team. Peer reviewers generally assume that you will be more competitive if you have already competed successfully for grant support. This impression is further strengthen, if you have published the results of such efforts.

11. Have you served as a key member of a research team?

If you have served as key member of a research team, then you are better prepared to accomplish the proposed work, meet agency reporting requirements, manage a research team, and produce reliable research results.

12. Have you been involved in the development of grant applications?

If you have developed a grant application before, then you have a basis for developing more competitive proposals. You should review your prior experience in terms of the strengths and weaknesses of earlier proposals; all weaknesses should be carefully addressed. Colleagues should be consulted for informal review of all subsequent proposals.

13. Do you feel capable of evaluating your own research work?

The ability to be self critical is essential, not only to the quality of the grant application you submit, but also to ensure a greater degree of thoughtfulness and thoroughness in your work.

14. Are you familiar with statistical software (e.g., SPSS, SAS, BMDP)?

Your education and experience should have acquainted you with a number of tools that are indispensable for research. Among these are statistical tools.

15. Can you develop a persuasive argument?

Your grant application must be a carefully crafted document that will capture the peer reviewers attention demonstrating your thoughtfulness and skill in presenting a readable, comprehensive science plan that, if funded, will result in a worthwhile contribution to your field. You must demonstrate your enthusiasm for the proposed research, the significance of the work to the field, and the power of your design to provide reliable data.

16. Can you demonstrate you ability to manage a research project?

Management skills are critical, especially when they are considered in the context of your proposed research design. You must be able to demonstrate your ability to select and manage key personnel, hire consultants, administer a consortium arrangement, respond to agency requirements, monitor the budget, and conduct all aspects of the proposed science.

Do You Have the Resources to Apply for a Grant?

1. Do you know how to notify your institutional administrators responsible for grant-management issues?

Most institutions offer some degree of grants activity support, yet surprisingly many new researchers are not aware of this support. You will need to know not only the process for review of human subjects, animal welfare, bio-safety, or bio-hazard protection issues, but also the sign-off and budget management procedures at your institution.

2. Do you know how to notify the IRB (Human Subjects / Animal Welfare) committee at your institution?

Your full understanding of the IRB Committee (Internal Review Board) and how it functions is critical. When used properly, the IRB will ensure that you are in compliance with protection regulations regarding research subjects or procedures and can also provide a first-level test of your idea before it leaves your institution.

3. Have you identified external resources necessary to support the development of a research application?

You should be fully aware of all possible avenues of support before you actually submit your application to an agency. By doing so, you will not only increase your chances of getting funded, but direct your proposal to the most appropriate funding agency.

4. Have you prepared a human consent form?

The use of human subjects in a research design requires that the researcher provide full and open disclosure of all possible risks to the health, welfare, or legal standing of the participant. Researchers should be aware of PHS requirements for the protection of human subjects. Failure to provide such assurance can lead to an unfundable proposal.

5. Have you analyzed space, computing needs, and availability?

You should develop a list of required resources, such as office or lab space, equipment including computers and office furniture, transportation, personnel, etc. Answering some of the following questions may prove helpful: Have you identified colleagues who will serve as informal reviewers of your draft proposal? Have you located secretarial support for the development of a publication quality application? If you are proposing to use specific software tools, have you a clear understanding of the benefits and limitations of these tools? Have you negotiated for space and equipment with your administrator?

6. Are you aware of your institutional sign-off procedures?

Knowledge of the sign-off procedure is critical because it demonstrates your level of awareness about the strengths and limitation of your own institution. Armed with this knowledge you can begin to develop a timeline with critical dates for IRB and budget review, receipt of support letters from collaborators or consultants, completion of final draft for informal review, and, submission to the funding agency.

7. Can you identify personnel who are likely to contribute to your grant application?

Determining key personnel resources and support staff is your responsibility, and your ability to manage this team will affect the overall competitiveness of your grant application.

8. Is your institution going to give you the backing (e.g., in-kind or cost-sharing contributions) for your proposed research?

When institutional backing is absent or not demonstrated, you will greatly diminish your chances for getting funded, since the review committee will most likely conclude that poor institutional support inevitably leads to limited research success.

9. Can you negotiate time away from teaching obligations?

Release time from teaching usually means full institutional support for your proposed research. It also demonstrates a commitment on your part to argue strongly for the importance of your research to the institution's mission.

10. Can you determine costs (e.g., level of effort, subject costs) associated with the proposed research?

The more clearly and convincingly you can cost out your proposed research with justifications, the better your chances of getting funded. You need to remember that you are being judged by experienced grantees who have constructed many budgets and subsequently conducted research within the confines of these budgets.

What About Your Research Design?

1. Can you correctly identify dependent and independent variables?

Variables must be correctly identified in the body of the proposal.

2. Will you use standardized data collection instruments?

If yes, what instruments are you planning to use and on what basis were they selected? If no, have you reviewed the thousands of well-validated data-collection instruments and are your reasons for not selecting any of these well grounded?

3. Are you going to create a new scale or measure?

New investigators should avoid the development of new scales. Given the number of validated instruments, the risks involved in the development of new instruments, and the advanced level of experience required to develop new instruments, it is recommended that the use of existing instruments be carefully considered. Should your design require the development of a new data-collection instrument, you should seek a Co-Principal Investigator with the requisite experience.

4. Do you have proven access to your subject population?

Failure to secure proven access to your proposed research population will negatively effect your funding potential. Letters attached to the application from the those who have the authority (e.g., school principals hospital administrators, prison administrators, and clinic directors) to grant you access to the required subjects is recommended.

5. Is the estimated duration of your proposed research realistic?

Have you carefully considered the amount of time it will take to recruit key personnel; train data-collection personnel; collect data, deliver interventions; clean, code and enter data; and begin analysis? You should consider the development of a timeline containing critical points in the research process. You should discuss this timeline with more experienced investigators who can help strengthen your projections.

6. Is your research question(s) clearly stated?

Develop a clearly articulated statement regarding your research question(s).

7. Is your sample size(s) adequate?

Have you carefully considered the size of your sample and have you considered recruitment, non-compliance, retention, and related issues and the effect on sample size? Have you considered using a Power Calculation to determine the appropriate sample size? Conversely, is your sample so large as to increase the cost of the study and place at potential risk more subjects than would be required to produce a statistically significant outcome.

8. Are your inclusion criteria adequate?

When designing your sampling strategy, have you carefully constructed the criteria for inclusion such that those entering into the study are appropriate to your design? Are the requirements so stringent that obtaining an adequate number will be too difficult?

9. Have you identified any potential risk to subjects participating in your research?

A critical element in the development of your research proposal is the consideration of potential risk to subjects participating in the study. All efforts must be taken to reduce potential risks and an informed consent form should be prepared according to the PHS guidelines.

10. Does the geographic location of your research matter?

Have you considered the effects of region or location on your research? Will research results be effected by the geographic setting? And have you provided a discussion of potential effects?

11. Will the data derived from this research experience be published?

Since the publication of your research results is a critical step in the scientific process, have you identified potential journals and have you contacted journal editors for publication instruction and interest? Have you created a schedule for the development of publications and have authorship considerations been discussed with key members of the research team?

12. Could you defend your research design?

Your research design should be based on the nature of your research question(s) and the aim(s) of the proposed work. If other designs are likely to provide reliable data, why have you selected this particular approach? How does it differ from other options? What are its strengths relative to the other strategies? And are all aspects of the design in agreement with each other?

How Significant Is Your Research?

1. Will your research lay the foundation for future work?

The aim of your research should be to push forward the knowledge in your research discipline. Peer reviewers must be able to see the significance of the work you are proposing and the power of the design to produce reliable results that will result in scientific articles. Thus, peer reviewers look for proposed work that extends the knowledge base, makes contributions to a particular discipline, and is of utility to the funding agency.

2. Is this research central to your career?

To be successful in getting a grant award, your proposed research must be central to your training, experience, and career interests. Otherwise, you suggest that you are perhaps "chasing research dollars" rather than pushing forward the boundaries of your field. Also when you suggest that you are working somewhat afield from your experience, then greater justification must be provided in the body of the application. In some instances, you may need to find a Co-Principal Investigator who has the requisite credentials in the proposed research area.

3. Will your research make a significant contribution to your field?

The degree of significance your proposed research will have greatly plays into the fundability of your project. For example, replication studies may be safer, but they tend to leave peer reviewers with less enthusiasm than they might have for more original work. Moreover, the proposal must address the mission of the funding institution; that is, the results of your work should provide answers to questions of interest to the funding source.

4. Can you state the distinctive/unique aspect(s) about your research idea?

To be successful in getting a grant award, your proposed work must stand out as distinctive or unique. This is sometimes difficult if you're proposing a replication study. However, even here you should point out how your work is extends previous efforts. For example, one of your methods may differ from prior work -- this difference and its possible contribution should be made clear in the body of your proposal.

5. Does your research fit into a larger research/scientific context?

The research context includes prior work that you and your colleagues have done in the proposed research area. It also includes published articles and books that outline theoretical positions. Together these entities form a broad research context within which you are likely to be judged. If your work falls outside this research context, then greater justification is required on your part.

6. Will your research lead to new knowledge or an original contribution?

New knowledge can be considered an original contribution to your research field. It is often reflected in peer reviewed articles and presentations in national conferences. If your proposed research does not speak to the potential for contributions of new knowledge, then your priority scores may be adversely affected.

7. Are the tools/methods/resources you have selected likely to contribute to the quality of your research?

The questions, tools, methods, and resources must all be carefully matched to insure that each is appropriate and that each is interconnected to other aspects of the proposal. For example, an inappropriate data collection instrument or methodology may totally discredit your research idea. In other instances, you may have a very sound proposal, but lack the personal or institutional resources to carry out the proposed work, leaving you again in an unfundable position.

8. Does your research show evidence of building on prior experience/theories?

By building on prior experience or theories, you not only suggest that you are current in your field but that you will not be beginning your research in isolation from other scientists working in your area. Such research requires a through knowledge of the field; the ability to interpret the results of other work and the expertise to derive new questions, approaches, or methods.

9. Is the proposed research timely?

This question is related to the earlier question of "new knowledge," but here the issue is the timeliness of proposed research. Timeliness is often, but not always, defined by the institute's agenda as stated in its grants announcements. But timeliness can also be dictated by the research field, especially when new theories and/or methods are prevalent. In either case you must address how timely your proposal is and define its expected impact.

10. Will your research result in publication within the next year?

This question addresses the issues of currency and outcome. If you cannot demonstrate that your work is likely to be reflected in publications and presentations, then you risk an unfavorable review. One of the hallmarks of the scientific process is the publication of results so that your peers have an opportunity to review your work and, in some cases, test the adequacy and accuracy of your work by proposing replication studies.

11. Does your research address the mission of the funding agency?

Matching your research agenda with the institute's mission will result in a more favorable review score because it suggests that you have carefully considered the relevance of your work to the needs of the funding agency. Sometimes this match is reflected in a response to a grants announcement; other times it may be reflected in an unsolicited proposal that significantly extends the boundaries of your field, but falls within the scope of the funding agency.

What Do You Know about the Funding Agency?

1. Can you name two researchers in your field that are currently supported by an NIH grant?

Knowing others who have been funded by NIH will not improve your chances of getting funded, but it will increase your knowledge of how the funding agency operates, what is expected in terms of the presentation of your science in an application, and what are the most current research findings in your area. This knowledge can then be used skillfully as you prepare your grant application.

2. Can you name one researchers in your field who serves on an NIH peer review committee?

Though you are not targeting your research proposal to the needs of the reviewer, you could profit from knowing members who serve on such committees. This knowledge will help to achieve the correct level of detail and argument in your proposal, as well as sensitize you to the needs and expectations of the committee.

3. Can you name a project officer at NIH?

Your Project Officer can be a valuable resource in helping you to shape your research ideas into a fundable project. The Project Officer should provide technical assistance including pre-proposal review.

4. Can you name a Scientific Review Administrator at NIH?

The Scientific Review Administrator is responsible for the peer review process, from assignment of reviewers through the actual review and the development of the Summary Statement.

5. Do you have access to NIH's research announcements?

This information is useful in understanding the objectives of the Institute. For updated or expanded information, contact the officer responsible for sponsored research activities at your institution or the university library for access to online search capabilities of the NIH Guide to Grants and Contracts.

6. Do small grants have to go to Council for review?

Small grants are reviewed by the IRG committees, but are not sent to the usual second level of review -- that is, the Council. This policy allows the Institute to expedite the funding of these grants.

7. Does NIH fund State institutions?

NIH grant awards can be made to State agencies. Usually special forms are required and in some cases special regulations may apply. Potential applicants from State agencies should contact their supervisor regarding application requirements.

8. Do the NIH Scientific Review Administrators make funding decisions?

The Scientific Review Administrator's role is limited to the review of the grant application. Funding decisions are made at the program level, generally by the appropriate Division Director in consultation with other program staff.

9. Will NIH accept a pre-proposal?

It is recommended that you prepare a pre-proposal. Project officers are required to review these documents and provide technical assistance where appropriate.

10. Will NIH want to invest its resources in your particular proposal?

This can be determined by reviewing general research announcements, which can be found in the Information System. This information is useful in weighing your proposed research in light of the objectives of an institute.

11. Do you know how to contact NIH project officers?

If you can contact a project officer, you will be able to get more information on how the institute functions and answers to some questions that pertain to your proposed research.

12. Do you know NIH's current budget and research priorities?

Budget and research priorities can be determined from the general and specific grant announcements, as well as from the Project Officer. By knowing this information, you can better gauge the appropriateness and timeliness of your proposed research.
