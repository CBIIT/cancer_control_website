Discussion

Study designs are usually classified in terms of the temporal nature of the data and of the number of assessments that are made of each subject. A number of more specific strategies have been discussed elsewhere and might be of interest to the reader (although none of these discussions will be all-encompassing because researchers as a group are highly creative). Unfortunately, only a discussion of general study designs is within the scope of this presentation.

Retrospective Studies

These data can be used to identify variables that are related to the condition of interest. In retrospective studies, the current condition of the subject is often known to the person who is being interviewed (e.g., the person smokes and has lung cancer). In this type of study, a self-selection bias has almost always occurred; that is, long ago the person may have chosen the lifestyle that contributed to the condition that the person has. Relatedly, the factors that may actually be contributors to the outcome are difficult to measure because the factors were present years in the past, and the accuracy of long-term memory may be questionable.

First, cases—individuals who have the condition of interest—are identified and a comparison sample, which is the control group, is selected. To make a valid comparison, however, the control group should resemble the cases as much as possible. The controls are usually matched to the cases on one or more variables. For an example, when matching on gender, if a female case is admitted to the study, a female control is also obtained. If matching on age and gender, if a female case is recruited who is 65 years old, a female control who is of a similar age, usually within a couple of years, is obtained. Sometimes overmatching can be accomplished to increase the sample size. To utilize overmatching, two or three control subjects are recruited for every one case. Rarely is there an situation when overmatching the other way—two or three cases for each control—will occur because usually the number of cases is almost always more limited than the number of controls.

The term overmatching has been used in another context, however. In this framework, too many matching variables are used, and finding a comparable control may be difficult or impossible. For example, finding a match would be difficult if a case were an 85 year old man and the criteria on which to match were: no physical disabilities, high socioeconomic status, and plays tennis five times a week.

Several investigators have suggested that long term memory is not especially reliable and can bias the results of a retrospective study. For example, subjects with severe medical conditions may wish to "help" the investigator explain the reason that they have developed their condition, especially if the subject has knowledge of the purported relationship between the risk factor in question and his or her condition. For instance, a recovering alcoholic who suffers a relapse may blame a major life event or a series of life events for the relapse.

The benefits of a retrospective study are that they are usually quick to conduct and are relatively inexpensive. The amount of time and expense increases, however, with the difficulties associated with choosing control subjects if a matching scheme is used in the design. The disadvantages are:

  • Potential for self-selection bias
  • Bias or unreliability of memory
  • Lack of evidence for a causal relationship because determining which condition occurred first is difficult
  • Difficulty in finding appropriate controls
  • Unsuitability for rare exposures to a potential risk factor

Cross-sectional Studies

Cross-sectional studies are conducted to generate a "snapshot" of the current study sample, and thus presumably the target population. In these studies, data are typically collected across several groups of people. A typical example of cross-sectional studies is a community survey. Note that the "community" can be fairly specific and can consist of students in a school system, university faculty, everyone living in this country, or residents living close to a nuclear reactor. The information gathered in these studies might include retrospective data such as "How often in the last month did you exercise for at least 30 minutes?"

Well-designed cross-sectional studies are often useful when estimating incidence (the number of new cases in a population) or prevalence (the number of cases, regardless of when onset occurred) in a population. A study of incidence might include collecting serologic data to determine individuals who have been exposed to HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus). For prevalence, a cross-sectional study might be conducted to identify the percentage of the population who reports illicit drug usage or patterns of heavy consumption of alcohol. Note, however, that sensitive information—including information about deviant behavior—is often difficult to collect on a reliable basis in any study. Most individuals who engage in that behavior are hesitant to admit to their behaviors because of a desire to appear within social norms or to avoid legal hassles. In cross-sectional studies, individuals may be unlikely to provide this type of data to a stranger, particularly if the subject has nothing to gain but, as far as the subject knows, prosecution for the illicit behavior.

A cross-sectional study like any study design has advantages and disadvantages. The advantages of a cross-sectional study are that they can be conducted quickly and the cost per subject is relatively low. Of course, the study becomes more expensive if the data collection instrument is long, face-to-face interviewers are used instead of phone interviews, or a large number of subjects is recruited into the study. The drawbacks are that no information is collected from which causality can be demonstrated, and for some items, (e.g., sensitive items), the respondent may tend to provide socially desirable answers rather than valid ones.

Longitudinal

Typically, longitudinal studies provide data of the highest quality. The data can often be used as evidence of a temporal relationship (e.g., perhaps indicating a causalrelationship, because data are collected from subjects at two or more times during the study. Unfortunately, data from these studies often take years to collect, requiring a considerable commitment from the research staff and the funding agency. Therefore, the expense is much higher than that for other studies in terms of financial cost and time required to collect data.

Several types of longitudinal studies can be conducted. First, a longitudinal study does not have to be prospective—that is, the study may not necessarily be designed to follow subjects until a specific outcome occurs. Information may be obtained from the subjects at baseline, and again at one or more follow-ups. The baseline or some of the early follow-up data are assessed to determine if they are predictive of the outcome of interest, such as the development of cancer ten to fifteen years after the study began. In this case, the original study may have been to supply investigators with cross-sectional data, which happened to be useful as baseline data when follow-up could be accomplished. Of course, the outcome variable may be defined as morbidity, mortality, remission, recidivism, or change in behavior or attitude.

Prospective studies usually have a more specific purpose than an ordinary longitudinal study. Typically, prospective studies are those in which subjects are followed over a specified period of time or until an event of interest occurs. An example could be one of a drug abuse treatment program in which success is defined as abstinence for two years after treatment; the investigators maintain contact with such subjects for a period until two years or until evidence is obtained that the subject has not been abstinent. Thus, at the end of the second year, all subjects can be defined as "successes" or "failures."

On a more experimental level (one in which the researcher manipulates conditions in the study) clinical trials can be considered the most desirable type of prospective study when assessing the efficacy of some treatment because of the experimental component into the design. In a clinical trial, two or more groups of subjects are given different treatments, followed over a period of time, and the groups are compared to one another on outcome. The decision about which subject receives a given mode of treatment should be made by some sort of randomization procedure—that is each subject has an equal chance of being assigned into each group.

Randomization should help ensure that both groups are at least roughly equal on background characteristics. Investigators should compare the groups on background variables to confirm that the groups are indeed not significantly different from one another in terms of variables that may confound the results. If the data collection portion of the study is not over when imbalances between groups are noted, group differences may disappear with the addition of more subjects. If differences do not disappear by the time the data have been collected, investigators may be able to adjust for the potentially confounding variables in a multivariate equation. To do so, however, a statistical expert should be consulted to avoid problems with misinterpretation of the results of the analysis.

Clinical trials often consist of subjects who are assigned into either a treatment or a control group. The control or comparison group often receives no treatment although in some studies, e.g., those with medical outcomes, withholding treatment would be considered unethical. When possible, another good strategy is to have three groups—treatment, no treatment control, and current preferred treatment. Thus, the placebo effect—when improvement is found with no treatment, a phenomenon that usually cannot be explained but that very often occurs in clinical trials—is controlled with the inclusion of the no treatment group, and the new treatment can be directly compared to the group that receives the current treatment of choice. If more "treatments of choice" exist, then a commensurate number of control groups can be added, as long as the investigators are able to administer the comparison treatments as effectively as possible.

To help remove the possibility of bias, most investigators attempt to incorporate a blind design into the clinical trial. That is, neither the subject nor the investigator should know the subject's treatment status; which is called a double blind design. If either the researcher or the subject has information about the treatment status, the design is single blind; if both the researcher and the subject know, the study is not blind at all.

The reason for a blind study is to eliminate the possibility for bias in the reporting or the recording of data. Obviously, however, for medical or behavioral or psychosocial interventions, double blind or single blind studies are rarely conducted because the subject or the investigator usually knows the treatment regimen. In such cases, the subject should be evaluated by an independent researcher who is blind to the focus of the study and the treatment regimen to which the subject has been assigned.

Another potential problem with longitudinal studies—particularly clinical trials—is that an investigator may "peek" at the data prior to ending the study. Such a temptation on the part of the researcher is understandable, particularly when data have been collected over a period of years—the investigator is eager to examine the results and may wants to begin ensuring the data that have been obtained are as error free as possible. Unfortunately when peeking occurs, the observed results may not be as attractive as the investigator had hoped, and some consideration may be given to ending the trial prematurely. Unless the data are absolutely overwhelming (especially if the experimental treatment has a significantly adverse effect on subjects), however, the study should be continued until the scheduled termination date. The subsequent observations that will provide follow-up may yield data that are so highly consistent with the investigator's hypothesis that the significance of the findings may be shaped by the final few observations. Researchers may be well advised, however, that if the final few cases greatly influence the overall findings, the significance of the results should be considered highly tenuous because findings are expected to be reasonably consistent throughout the course of project.

Finally, in longitudinal studies, attrition of subjects can adversely influence the data. Attrition occurs when subjects no longer provide information to the researcher because they have dropped out of the study, e.g., because they have moved, become discontent with their involvement in the study, or have died. In some types of studies, attrition is not likely to be random. An example would be a drug intervention study; subjects who refuse follow-up may do so to avoid embarrassment or fear of prosecution because they have begun to use drugs again.

One way to reduce attrition is to offer a cash payment or some other reward for the subject's continued participation in the study. Sometimes, a free physical examination or some other service that is related to the study design can be offered relatively inexpensively and with a reasonable amount of success. These incentives will not, however, ensure the validity of information from those individuals who would have otherwise dropped out of the study.

Researchers should make every effort to contact subjects who have dropped out of the study to avoid the obvious criticism that the final sample, the one containing subjects with follow-up data, is not the same as the sample that began the study. Sometimes collecting data from individuals who dropped out of the study may be accomplished by simply redoubling the effort required for a routine follow-up. Baseline characteristics of subjects who remained in the study should always be compared to those of individuals who dropped out of the study to identify the characteristics that are dissimilar between the two groups. However, investigators should keep in mind that, because the power of the test would be unacceptably low, statistical tests are not likely to be significant if the number of people who dropped out of the study is small. Consequently, researchers should not conclude, if the numbers in either of the groups is small, that the group who did not participate in the follow-up is no different from the group remaining in the study.

Historical Prospective

Historical prospective studies have some of the same characteristics of a prospective study while reducing the amount of time that must be invested. Usually, in these studies archival data is utilized to provide much of the data. Archival data are those which are obtained from documents that typically exist for some reason other than research purposes—such as medical records. Subjects are often identified based on their status in these records and followed over a period of time to yield results similar to from a longitudinal study.

For example, an investigator wishes to determine if the drop-out rate for urban high school students is related to drug involvement. Examination of police or school records could be used to identify individuals with drug histories; thus the rate of these individuals dropping out of school can be compared to that of students in other categories.

Unfortunately, archival records may not contain all of the information the investigator needs to adequately test the hypothesis, or the data may be maintained at the archival source in such a way that using the available information in data analysis would be impossible. Another potential problem is that archival records do not necessarily contain valid or complete information because the individuals who recorded the information may not have been especially meticulous. Moreover, the quality of the data in an archival source may be biased; individuals with particular problems or characteristics may be more likely to have incomplete records, for example. Finally, attrition can occur in these studies, too, with individuals who would have been categorized in one group no longer accessible to the investigator.
