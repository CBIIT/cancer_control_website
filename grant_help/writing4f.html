Data Management

Topics

  1. Types of Data
    • Nominal data has no natural order (examples are ethnicity, gender, etc.).
    • Ordinal data can be ordered from "good" to "bad," "first" to "last," etc. and usually has a restricted range.
    • Interval data also has order, but the points on the scale are more numerous than in an ordinal scale—an example is the Fahrenheit scale.
    • Ratio scales are similar to interval scales except that ratio scales have a true zero point—an example is the Kelvin scale or timed events.
    • Miscellaneous information about types of data include:
      • Data can never be converted "up" in quality
      • Ordinal/nominal sometimes called categorical variables
      • Ordinal/interval/ratio data sometimes called continuous

  2. Dependence Versus Independence
    • Often variables are identified as such only for convenience when using certain statistical packages
    • Causality is never determined in a cross-sectional or retrospective study

  3. Options for Missing Data
    • Strongly consider throwing subjects out of the analyses if they have missing values on the variables of interest.
    • Investigators can replace the missing values by using the average, imputed from subjects that do not have missing data.
    • Researchers should also consider reducing missing values by more aggressively following up on "lost" subjects.

  4. Bias
    • Bias is always systematic and usually runs throughout the data set experimenter bias can result if the investigator:
      • Expects an outcome and "finds" it in the data
      • Biases the subjects' responses
    • The subject may also provide biased responses when
      • The item has a socially desirable response
      • The subject perceives the is expecting a certain answer (experimenter expectancy effects)
    • Some ways to reduce bias include
      • Using objective instruments
      • Using double blind studies methods

  5. Error
    • Error is random
    • Error may occur:
    • In instruments
    • In coding
    • In data entry: (a) out of range values (age of >100) (b) impossible combinations—e.g., high rate of men in breast cancer group
    • In programming: (a) missing values are sometimes coded as 99 but not identified as such in the program (b) categorization of a group may not be programmed correctly
    • Investigators seeking a grant might want to describe how efforts to reduce error will be (or have been) accomplished. For example:
    • Obtain frequencies of relevant variables to identify out-of range values or values that are missing
    • Enter the data twice into different data sets & search for discrepancies between the two data sets with a computerized "compare" procedure
    • Utilize computer programs (a) for data collection (b) to transfer data from one program to other (c) to analyze data

Discussion

Data

The quality of the data collected in a research project is inextricably related to the worthiness of that project. Researchers should generally not only know the data from their research project well but also understand tenets about data. Much of the following discussion is related to concepts that will be important during the data analysis. To convince a grant review committee that the potential grantee knows how to conduct research and data analysis, the applicant should demonstrate a knowledge about data and the selection of data analytic methods.

Types of Data

The classification of data is necessary to conceptualize the quality of the data and to be able to select the appropriate statistical test for the analysis.

Nominal data have no natural order (e.g., cannot be ranked from good to bad or first to last) and thus are strictly categorical. Examples of nominal data are: gender (male/female), ethnicity (Hispanic, Black, White), source of subject (urban/rural, school A/school B, etc.), or treatment status (experimental, control).

Ordinal data can also be classified as categorical data, but the subgroups have some type of sequence. For instance, a person's condition can be classified as "good," "fair," or "poor." Individuals may respond to a question as either "strongly agree," "agree," "neither agree nor disagree," "disagree," or "strongly disagree." Because ordinal scales have some gradation in quality, they usually provide more information than the "either/or" classification of nominal scales. However, the range of ordinal scales is usually quite limited—from 2 levels to perhaps 7 or more levels; the more levels the scale has, the more the data can be considered continuous data.

A type of scale that provides more information than ordinal data is that of interval data. The points on the scale are much more numerous than on an ordinal scale (making a continuous scale), and the increments between the data points are consistent throughout the scale. Examples of interval scales are: the Fahrenheit scale, calendar dates, and scores from various psychosocial tests.

Ratio scales provide the most information of any type of measure, and are similar to interval scales with the exception that a ratio scale has a true zero point. Examples of ratio data are: the Kelvin scale measures of distance, and timed events.

General considerations about data types include the fact that scales can be converted "down" in quality. That is, the points on an interval scale can be collapsed to form an ordinal scale that has a more restricted range of data (e.g., test scores of individuals can be classified into those who scored in the highest third, the middle third, and the lowest third of the sample). Such manipulation can be useful for examining nonlinear relationships or differences between the "highest" and the "lowest" scores. However, a scale can never be converted "up" in quality; a nominal scale can never yield ordinal data, for instance. The exception is when the scale was originally interval or ratio and recategorized, in which case the initial values may be restored to an ordinal scale.

Dependence Versus Independence

Using computers to analyze data has become common. Calculating the results of statistical tests by hand or by using a calculator is rarely recommended; time is wasted and errors in the analysis are more likely to occur. However, the alternative use of computer assisted statistical packages has some drawbacks, too. One of these involves the nomenclature in which "dependent" and "independent" variables must often be identified to conduct the analysis. Unfortunately, investigators may fall into the trap of accepting the fact that the outcome variable, which is almost always used as the "dependent" variable, is truly dependent on the influence of independent variables. As noted elsewhere, however, causal data are never obtained from cross-sectional studies, and therefore, the outcome variable should not be considered dependent on the independent variables. Researchers who overstate the confidence with which their data are causal may be more likely to obtain lower priority scores when submitting a grant.

Missing Data

The amount of missing data that the study suffers is often annoying and may place the validity of the study in question. Subjects may find a question offensive or confusing and decide not to respond to that item; sometimes an entire page of questions is inadvertently skipped; the person collecting or coding the data may have been careless; an interview is prematurely terminated by the subject because of time constraints, or parts of the questionnaire are mixed with other papers and lost. Investigators usually have to make some decision about what to do when missing data is identified.

In general, if a subject has contributed no data on a variable that will be used in a particular analysis, dropping that subject from the analysis is probably advisable. However, including such subjects in the analysis can be accomplished by 1) inserting the mean score as that person's value or 2) estimating the scores with regression analysis from values obtained from nonmissing subjects. These two methods, however, are weak because they assume that the person with the missing value is probably similar to the rest of the sample. Such an assumption is perhaps a giant leap of faith because the rest of the sample was different in at least the fact that they did not have a missing value on the scale or item of interest.

Occasionally, missing data can be captured by increasing the vigor with which it is pursued. That is, if the data have come from archival records, other sources that provide the same type of information may be consulted, or another data collection person may be sent to the source of the data to reexamine the records that are incomplete. Missing data in longitudinal studies are a particular problem because of attrition. Sometimes in longitudinal studies, missing data can be reduced by redoubling efforts to track down the subjects who were lost to follow-up.

Bias

Investigators are often slow to recognize the possibility that their data suffers or will suffer from bias because of a design flaw. Bias is a condition that causes the results to be skewed either toward or away from significance. Bias is always systematic, consistently influencing the data one way or the other, and permeates or runs throughout the data set.

Although hardly ever intended, the experimenter or investigator can defeat the purpose of the study—providing usable data to the field—by introducing bias into the data in any number of ways. The most obvious form of experimenter bias is that the investigator expects to find a certain relationship and does find such a relationship without support from objective instruments. For example, a study is conducted by a proponent for a certain type of drug intervention therapy. The researcher uses a widely accepted scale of drug abuse which is administered verbally and is used in this study to assess the patient's pre- and post-treatment condition. Particularly if 1) the treatment is found effective, 2) the researcher conducts the interviews, and 3) no control group is used, critics will almost surely raise the question of bias. The investigator, who is very closely attached to the hypothesis, may well give the client the "benefit of the doubt" on various items when the post-treatment interview is given, inflating the observed effect of the treatment. Similar criticisms may also arise if the researcher employs interviewers, if these individuals are trained by the investigator. The researcher may be able to transfer biases to those who will conduct the interview. Despite using a control group, if the interviewers are not blind to the subject's treatment status, a charge of bias, which will not be easily answered, may be leveled at the study.

Moreover, experimenter bias may also exist when the researcher accurately records the subject's responses. Investigators may influence the subjects' responses by posing items in such a way to elicit a desired response. The cues provided by the researcher need not be verbal, with facial changes' or certain behaviors' having the potential for prejudicing the data.

Subjects, too, can be the source of bias as indicated by the above example where responses may be more influenced by external cues. Typically, items that may solicit biased responses are those that may allow socially desirable answers. Deviant behavior such as illicit drug use may be denied by a subject to continue to appear socially acceptable to the investigator. Similarly, if the subject suspects that a certain response is expected, the subject will often try to accommodate the researcher by providing the perceived desired answer. Such a problem may be especially found in studies that are designed to assess the efficacy of some treatment. When the post-test data are collected, the subject knows the treatment was administered and what the outcome was expected to be, and thus the subject renders the expected response. Again, such an issue may be magnified if the treatment is one for a deviant behavior such as drug abuse or, usually to a lesser extent, for a behavior that is merely seen to be undesirable, such as cigarette smoking. In such cases, investigators may do well to assess the treatment outcome through different means, rather than relying solely on self-report.

When developing a grant application or writing a paper for a study in which bias may be an issue, researchers should emphasize the steps taken to minimize the bias. Double-blind designs should be used whenever possible, and the fact that a blind design was or is going to be used should be highlighted for reviewers when appropriate. If utilizing interviewers is part of the design, the level of training that they will receive, their experience conducting interviews, and, if possible, their disinterest in the outcome of the study should be stressed. Researchers should chose instruments that obtain objective responses, and again the grant proposal or paper should provide evidence that such will be the case.

Error

Two sources of error that occur in virtually every research project are caused by the instrument itself (e.g., the items are ambiguously worded or the response categories are inappropriate) or the subject (e.g., items were misread, problems of memory). The results from most instruments that were designed to produce the most marginally sensitive results contain at least some error because as sensitivity increases, specificity decreases, which reduces the precision of the instrument. However, items that would be expected to be completely reliable (e.g., r=1.00) are often not. For example, when subjects are asked their gender at two points in time, a perfect correlation between the Time 1 and Time 2 administration would not be expected, even though the variable gender is typically considered to be constant. Again subjects may mistakenly respond with the wrong value.

Error can also arise during coding (e.g., the coder misinterprets or misreads a response) or data entry (e.g., the data entry person enters a typo or cannot read the code and guesses the response). The errors that occur after the subject has finished the interview can be addressed in several ways. Perhaps one of the most desirable is to have the subject enter responses directly into a computer file from prompts at a computer terminal. This method may be expensive in terms of computer resources—although if the subjects are mailed the diskette to be used on the subject's own computer, the cost would be minimal—the reduction in error and the increased integrity of the data set would be more assured. When such technology is not available to collect the subjects' responses directly, other means of protecting the quality of the data can be used, although here an assumption is being made that the data will be analyzed on a computer. In such a case, the most preferable method of ensuring data quality is that the data be entered twice into separate computerized datasets and the contents of the two datasets compared. Discrepancies between the two databases are quickly identified and the corrections can then be made. A less accurate but also less expensive method to examine the quality of the data is to merely examine the frequency counts and percentages of the response levels to 1) identify out of range values, such as an age of 95 years or 2) examine the prevalence of impossible combinations—a data set that had a high rate (e.g., > 1%) of male breast cancer patients should be suspect. When these values are identified, the original data collection sheets must be consulted to resolve the inaccuracies.

Because computers have become ubiquitous in research because they can be used to analyze large amounts of data quickly and effectively, errors in programming should be of concern. In some research projects, missing values are coded with an out of range value, such as "99", and a line will be included in a computer program will identify this value as "missing". Forgetting to identify these values as missing may yield some rather interesting, as opposed to valid, results when statistical tests are applied to the data because the computer package will treat "99" as a true numeric value. In this example, individuals in a treatment program may be found to have a median age of 25 when the group was obtained exclusively from a high school; the average age would have been inflated by including some 99 year old high school sophomores in the analysis. Another potential programming error includes misassigning individuals into the wrong group for analysis; this particular problem is more likely to be observed in programs that are especially complex.
